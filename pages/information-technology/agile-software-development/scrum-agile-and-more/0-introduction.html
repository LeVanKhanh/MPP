Introduction

Scrum, Agile and More

Published on: 28/03/2026

Scrum is a foundation, not a cure-all

Scrum is a minimalist yet powerful framework for tackling complex work through transparency, inspection, and adaptation. It gives the Scrum Team a clear structure to align around a Product Goal, deliver valuable Increments, and learn from reality in short cycles.

But Scrum is not designed to replace an organization's full operating system.

Scrum does not prescribe how to hire and grow people. It does not provide a full capability model, a performance management framework, career paths, organization-level decision rights, or a way to resolve every cross-functional power conflict. Scrum also does not offer a universal KPI formula for Product Owners, Scrum Masters, or Developers.

That is not a weakness in Scrum. It is an intentional boundary of a lightweight framework.

Scrum does not guarantee organizational success. It helps organizations see more clearly why they are not succeeding yet.

When the Product Goal is unclear, Scrum exposes a lack of product direction. When the backlog changes constantly, Scrum reveals weak decision quality or strategic instability. When Developers cannot self-manage, Scrum makes capability gaps, authority gaps, or poor working conditions visible. When events are held but no real learning happens, Scrum reveals form-over-substance behavior instead of true empiricism.

So Scrum is not the destination. It is the starting point for seeing the system more honestly.

Three Scrum accountabilities and one necessary supporting layer

In Scrum, the Scrum Team consists of three accountabilities: Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers. Each has a distinct focus, but none works in isolation. Value is created only when all three operate as one system.

Scope Primary Focus Core Question
Product Owner Product value direction Are we solving the right problem and prioritizing the right work?
Developers Building quality, sustainable Increments Can we turn goals into a usable product that can run reliably and evolve over time?
Scrum Master Improving team effectiveness and system conditions Is the team self-managing, learning, removing impediments, and improving continuously?
Individual development Growing human capability What should each person develop to contribute better and take on broader responsibilities?

The first three rows are Scrum accountabilities. "Individual development" is not a fourth Scrum accountability, and it does not replace shared team ownership.

It is an additional layer belonging to people management and capability development. Scrum requires a self-managing, cross-functional, capable team, but it does not define how an organization should build that capability. Organizations still need mechanisms to identify gaps, grow people, share knowledge, support mentoring, and provide meaningful career paths.

The Scrum Team is collectively accountable for value and Increments. The organization remains accountable for developing the capability that makes this possible.

An effectiveness lens: measure to learn, not to control

One of the biggest risks in Scrum adoption is applying old measurement habits: velocity, ticket counts, work hours, pull request counts, meeting counts, or visible busyness.

These data points are not useless. They can support forecasting, bottleneck detection, and trend observation. But once turned into performance KPIs, reward-penalty metrics, or team-comparison tools, they often distort behavior.

When velocity becomes the target, teams optimize story points. When ticket count becomes the target, people choose easy-to-close tasks. When pull request count becomes the target, Developers may produce formal but low-value micro-changes. When Scrum Masters are evaluated by meeting volume or impediment closure count, the role can shrink into facilitation and reporting. When Product Owners are judged only by release output, value is replaced by output.

That is why the measurement lens in this series follows one principle:

Do not use metrics to find someone to blame. Use metrics to understand what conditions the system lacks to create better value.

Every metric is a signal, not a verdict. A weak signal should trigger questions about root cause, context, constraints, and improvement actions, not immediate judgment of a person or team.

Four principles for a healthy measurement system

1. Observe trends, not snapshots

One Sprint missing the Sprint Goal, one release with defects, or one Retrospective with weak actions is not enough to conclude that a role or team is ineffective.

What matters is the trend across many Sprints and cycles: Is quality improving or declining? Are dependencies decreasing or growing? Are recurring issues being solved at the root? Is the team becoming more autonomous or more dependent?

2. No metric should be interpreted in isolation

Strong outcomes with rapidly growing technical debt may not be success. Faster flow with increasing escaped defects may not be improvement. Consistent Sprint Goal achievement with burnout, overtime, or rising knowledge concentration is not sustainable.

Any meaningful change should be read through at least four lenses:

  • Outcome: Did this change create the intended value or impact?
  • Flow: Is work moving steadily and transparently?
  • Quality: Are technical quality, operational reliability, and maintainability protected?
  • Sustainability: Can people and the system sustain these results over time?

3. Distinguish accountability from influence

A role can influence an outcome without being solely accountable for the full result.

For example, Product Owners are accountable for maximizing product value, but outcomes are also shaped by market dynamics, business strategy, marketing, pricing, user experience, and delivery capability. Developers are accountable for quality Increments, but they cannot remove every dependency, approval chain, or organization-level constraint alone. Scrum Masters can surface systemic impediments and drive change, but they do not own management decision rights.

So when evaluating effectiveness, always separate:

Concept Meaning
Accountability Who is primarily accountable within their role?
Influence Who can improve or worsen the outcome?
Decision right Who has the actual authority to decide and address the cause?
System owner Who owns the organizational conditions or barriers?

4. Every signal must lead to action

A dashboard that does not drive decisions, experiments, or improvements only creates more reporting.

Each important signal should have at least:

  • Observable evidence or data.
  • A hypothesis about root cause.
  • A person or group with authority to act.
  • A specific action or experiment.
  • A defined point for re-evaluation.

The goal is not to create more action items. The goal is to choose fewer, more meaningful improvements and follow through until impact is validated.

Topic Product Owner Developers Scrum Master Organization / People Manager

Product Backlog ordering Holds primary accountability Clarifies effort, risk, dependencies, and solution options Supports effective collaboration Respects the role and avoids bypassing the Product Owner
Increment and technical quality Clarifies value and needed acceptance Holds primary accountability under the Definition of Done Helps the Scrum Team improve how it works Provides tools, environment, and capability support

This table is not meant to create rigid borders for blame-shifting. It is intended to prevent a common failure mode: everyone is "responsible," but no one has clear authority and ownership to carry a problem through to resolution.

How to read the upcoming articles

The upcoming articles are not an attempt to invent a new Scrum formula or a one-size-fits-all KPI set.

Each article will focus on one scope:

  • Product Owner: how to observe value direction, reduce uncertainty, and make better product decisions.
  • Scrum Master: how to observe self-management, improvement capability, flow, and systemic impediments.
  • Developers: how to observe capability, technical quality, value collaboration, and delivery sustainability.
  • Individual development: how to assess personal capability growth without breaking the collaborative spirit of the Scrum Team.

These lenses should be used as an observation and dialogue system. They do not replace Scrum events, Product Goal, Sprint Goal, or Definition of Done. They also do not replace leadership responsibility for creating a healthy environment.

Scrum can make work more transparent. But sustainable value still depends on investment in capability, decision rights, technical quality, learning, and measurement discipline.

Scrum does not create a strong organization on behalf of people. It creates conditions where organizations can no longer hide the problems that prevent people from creating value.
