1. Scrum Team - Product Owner (PO)

Core issue: when the PO cannot provide clear value direction for the product.

Published on: 28/03/2026

Problem context: the PO role and Scrum expectations

A product does not fail because it lacks features.
It fails because it does not create real value.

According to the Scrum Guide, the Product Owner is accountable for maximizing product value by clearly understanding the Product Goal, market context, user needs, and priorities, then making that direction transparent to the Scrum Team. [1]

However, a practical question must be asked: what happens when the PO does not meet these expectations? What if direction is unclear, priorities are inconsistent, or value is misidentified?

In that case, should the development team continue executing requests passively, or should it also take responsibility for clarifying and protecting product value?

Decision-right: PO is accountable for value, but does not own every decision

The Product Owner is accountable for maximizing product value. However, that does not mean the PO must single-handedly own product strategy, budget, pricing, architecture, staffing, or all customer knowledge. A PO can only fulfill this accountability when the organization clarifies which decisions belong to the PO, which belong to business leaders, and which require co-creation with Developers.

Decision or Scope PO Primary Accountability Essential Collaboration System Owner / Authority Outside Team
Product Goal and Backlog Priority Clarify objectives, trade-offs, and priority order. Developers contribute feasibility, risk, solution options; Scrum Master supports transparency and empiricism. Business owner / leadership provides strategy, market context, funding, and legal constraints.
Product Discovery and Assumption Validation Define problems, desired outcomes, and which assumptions need testing. Developers, UX, data, sales, support, and users contribute evidence. Organization invests in customer access, data, research capability, and discovery time.
Technical Solution Clarify product value, constraints, and trade-offs. Developers hold primary accountability for turning ideas into quality Increments. Architecture/platform owner addresses standards, platform constraints, or cross-team dependencies when needed.
Urgent Work Between Sprints Clarify value and trade-offs with Product Goal/Sprint Goal. Developers assess delivery impact; Scrum Master supports transparent mechanisms. Manager or business owner decides when priority changes are strategic or mandatory.

This distinction avoids two extremes: PO reduced to "backlog manager" with no real authority, or PO expected to be accountable for outcomes without strategy, budget, customer access, or corresponding decision rights.

Three common team responses when PO direction is weak

When a PO cannot provide clear value direction, Scrum does not offer a concrete prescription. In practice, teams usually respond in three ways, and each response has distinct consequences.

1) Passive execution. This is the most common response. The development team keeps accepting requests and operates like an "execution team," avoiding debate to preserve pace and stability. In the short term, sprints finish, velocity looks stable, and backlog items keep moving. In the long term, however, the product loses direction, value is not optimized, and team ownership fades.

2) Unstructured pushback. Some teams challenge PO decisions when they see problems. This is a positive signal because the team starts caring about real product value. But if pushback is emotional, data-poor, or unsupported by a clear mechanism, conflict grows, collaboration declines, and decisions become slower and less consistent.

3) Compensating for the PO role. In more mature teams, developers do more than challenge. They actively clarify value by questioning goals, proposing solutions, and even reprioritizing work themselves. This may help in the short term, but over time it creates role drift: the team gradually carries PO responsibilities while the PO loses strategic direction ownership.

The gap that lies beyond Scrum

Scrum is not missing anything; it simply stops at the right boundary.
What comes next is an organizational capability challenge.

None of the three responses truly solves the root problem. Scrum exposes the gap, but filling that gap requires capabilities outside Scrum itself.

This leads to a deeper question: how can an organization develop PO capability, instead of forcing teams to constantly "adapt" to PO weaknesses?

From PO training to the measurement problem

If PO training works, that is valuable. Over time, the organization may have a PO who meets Scrum expectations.

But a more practical question remains: how do we know the training is truly effective? In other words, what evidence tells us a PO is actually meeting the role as intended?

Without a clear measurement system, evaluation usually falls back to perception: "the PO seems better," "the team feels smoother." These signals may be true, but they are not sufficient for decisions about continued investment, adjustment, or role change.

A common trap: measuring output instead of outcome

When organizations begin to measure PO effectiveness, they often fall into a familiar trap: measuring what is easy to see rather than what truly reflects value.

Scrum talks about "maximizing value" but does not define a fixed measurement model. Without care, measurement can drift off target from the beginning.

What should not be measured (or is often misread): number of backlog items written, requirement detail level, team velocity, and number of tasks completed in a sprint. Common issue: these indicators mostly measure output, not outcome.

Signals worth measuring and contextual interpretation

Better value-related signals include Product Goal clarity, consistency in prioritization, lead time from idea to impact (Idea-to-Impact), and user or stakeholder feedback.

Still, these indicators must be interpreted in context, not applied as a rigid formula.

There is no single "Scrum-standard KPI set" for PO performance. Instead of chasing one perfect number, organizations should return to foundational questions: does the team understand why this work matters, is what gets built actually used, and is the product moving closer to the Product Goal? The Scrum Guide introduces Product Goal to create focus for the Scrum Team around a larger value objective.[1]

Measuring PO effectiveness

Measurement is not about "proving whether the PO is good enough." It is about enabling decisions: continue investing, adjust, or change.

If measurement ends at evaluation and does not lead to action, then measurement itself creates no value.

Scrum does not prescribe a value measurement method, and that gap forces organizations to confront a harder question: what do we actually mean by "value"?

Practical PO observation framework: outcome, learning, flow, and decision quality

The goal of this framework is not to create a performance scorecard for the PO. It helps organizations see: whether the PO and Scrum Team are understanding the product better; whether decisions are increasingly evidence-based; and whether the system enables the PO to maximize value.

Four observation layers

Layer Core Question Observable Data or Evidence Purpose of Use
Outcome and Value Is the change creating the intended impact for users, the business, or reducing risk? Adoption, activation, retention, conversion, support demand, cost/risk reduction, user feedback. Validate Product Goal and inform next investment choices.
Discovery and Learning Is the PO reducing uncertainty before committing to large investments? Problem framing, assumptions, discovery evidence, prototypes/spikes, experiments, post-release learning. Avoid building large features based solely on opinion or authority.
Flow and Collaboration Is the PO clearing the path or creating friction for the Scrum Team? Backlog clarity, decision wait time, rework, scope churn, refinement quality, dependency visibility. Improve how the PO, Developers, and stakeholders work together.
Decision Quality and Enablement Does the PO make clear, consistent trade-offs with proper decision rights? Priority rationale, decision latency, outcome linkage, stakeholder alignment, data and customer access. Coach the PO and address organizational barriers.

1. Outcome and Value — Is the product moving closer to the Product Goal?

Outcome is not only revenue. Depending on the product, value can be users completing tasks better, reduced processing time, reduced risk, increased reliability, reduced support demand, or new business capability. Evidence-Based Management recommends using Key Value Areas as "lenses" to observe value, risk, and innovation capability, rather than seeking one single metric.[2]

Signal How to Read It Correctly Improvement Questions
Adoption / activation Do not just ask how many users touch the feature; ask whether the right users are using it to solve the intended problem. Is the feature easy to find, easy to understand, and does it truly address the pain point?
Outcome metric Read the trend together with quality, cost, and user feedback; avoid optimizing a single metric in isolation. When outcome improves, are we trading it off for defects, support burden, or operational cost?
Product Goal progress Observe whether Product Goal actually influences priorities and Sprint Goals over time. Does the backlog contain too many features unrelated to the Product Goal?
Post-release learning Feature release is the beginning of learning, not the end of delivery. Which assumptions proved true, false, or need adjustment after release?

2. Discovery and Learning — Reduce uncertainty before committing

The PO does not need to have all answers before Developers begin. But the PO needs to clarify the problem, desired outcome, assumptions being made, and how to test them. Effective product discovery invites Developers to participate early in assessing feasibility, risk, and alternative solutions, rather than simply handing down a feature specification to delivery.[3]

Signal How to Observe Red Flags
Problem clarity Do important backlog items describe the user, the problem, the context, and expected outcome? Items start with a pre-decided solution instead of stating what problem needs solving.
Assumption visibility Are assumptions about users, behavior, market, feasibility, or viability made explicit? The team debates solutions but does not know what is being assumed.
Experiment and early validation Are prototypes, spikes, usability checks, feature flags, staged rollouts, or small releases used when appropriate? The team builds large scope before collecting minimal evidence.
Learning loop Does the PO and team review evidence after experiments/releases and adjust the backlog as needed? Discovery only happens at the project start, not continuously from the market.

3. Flow and Collaboration — Is the PO clearing the path or creating bottlenecks?

Signal How to Read It Correctly Possible Action
Backlog clarity Do not count "ready" tickets; check whether important items have enough context for Developers to discuss, slice, and plan. Goal-first refinement, add context, clarify acceptance and dependencies.
Decision wait time Measure time work is blocked waiting for decisions, categorized by decision type and decision owner. Delegate decision rights, set decision policies, or establish escalation paths.
Rework due to misunderstanding Do not blame the PO or Developers; find gaps in discovery, refinement, feedback loops, or context changes. Joint refinement, early validation, document assumptions, maintain a decision log.
Scope churn Priority changes are not always bad; distinguish empiricism from whimsical changes. Link changes to evidence, Product Goal, and clear trade-offs.
Stakeholder bypass Observe when requests go directly to Developers or bypass the Product Backlog mid-Sprint. Establish intake paths, clarify urgent-work policy, and protect the Sprint Goal.

4. Decision Quality and Enablement — Are decisions clear and properly authorized?

Decision quality does not mean "decide fastest." A good decision fits the risk level, has a clear rationale, states the trade-offs explicitly, and includes a way to verify. When decision rights lie outside the PO, data should reveal this rather than evaluate the PO as though they have full authority.

Signal How to Observe Red Flags
Priority rationale Can the PO explain the goal, evidence, trade-offs, and the conditions requiring a priority shift? Priority only reflects who speaks loudest or unarticulated deadlines.
Decision latency Decision speed should match the risk level and decision type, not pursue absolute speed. Small decisions wait too long; large decisions change constantly without evidence.
Stakeholder alignment How well do stakeholders understand the Product Goal, priority logic, and the mechanism for handling requests? Stakeholders bypass the PO or constantly conflict over priorities.
Decision-right coverage Does the PO have the authority and access needed: priority decisions, data, customer feedback, budget boundaries? The PO is accountable for outcomes but lacks authority or information to match.

Product Owner Development Rubric

Level Description
Developing Primarily manages backlog and forwards requests; Product Goal, problem framing, and evidence do not consistently guide decisions.
Supported Beginning to clarify outcomes, identify assumptions, and consult with Developers/stakeholders, but needs support to establish discovery rhythm and clear trade-offs.
Reliable Uses Product Goal, evidence, and trade-offs to prioritize; collaborates well with Developers; creates feedback loops after release and is transparent about decisions needing escalation.
Multiplying Not only leads the product effectively but also builds product thinking, discovery capability, and evidence-based decision-making in other teams or among stakeholders.

This rubric serves coaching and capability development. It is not a performance score and should not be inferred from feature release count, velocity, or busyness.

Minimum viable Product Owner practice

New teams do not need to build large dashboards. In the first 2–3 Sprints, the PO and Scrum Team can start with four simple practices:

  • Each Sprint has a Sprint Goal clearly linked to the Product Goal or an assumption that needs testing.
  • Before Planning, important items are clarified using: problem, expected outcome, constraints, assumptions, and dependencies.
  • For each significant change, agree on at least one piece of evidence to check after release: adoption, feedback, error/support signals, or outcome metrics.
  • When priority changes, the PO articulates the evidence, trade-offs with current work, and who holds the decision right.

Guardrails against measurement abuse

Guardrail How to Apply
Do not reward-penalize using output Do not use feature release count, items written, velocity, or deadline compliance as direct KPIs for the PO.
Do not solely attribute outcome to PO Outcome is influenced by market, pricing, marketing, UX, sales, delivery capability, technical quality, and many factors outside the PO.
Evidence has context Each metric needs a baseline, appropriate cohort/timeframe, and qualitative evidence from users/stakeholders when needed.
Right to explain and counter The PO, Developers, and stakeholders should have a chance to present constraints, risks, and decision rights before conclusions are drawn.
Metrics lead to experiments When a weak signal appears, the goal is to adjust the hypothesis or try the next experiment, not to find someone to blame.

Illustration: Low adoption does not immediately mean the PO "chose wrong"

A feature is released but adoption is low one month later. The quick conclusion: the PO misread the need. A better observation separates the hypotheses:

Signal Hypothesis to Test Appropriate Action Co-Accountability
Users unaware feature exists Discoverability or communication insufficient. Check entry point, onboarding, in-product guidance, and messaging. PO + UX + Marketing/Support
Users try but do not return Solution does not address the pain point or quality/UX is insufficient. Interview users, analyze funnels, check errors/performance. PO + Developers + UX
Only one cohort is not using Target segment or rollout strategy needs adjustment. Analyze cohorts, adjust hypothesis, or scope rollout differently. PO + Data/Business owner
Outcome correct but operational cost high Short-term value is creating sustainability risk. Add technical/operational cost to trade-offs and backlog. PO + Developers + Manager

This case helps the PO and organization learn from evidence. It avoids treating adoption as an isolated verdict removed from product context, delivery quality, and user access capability.

From measurement to decision

Based on signals about outcomes, discovery, flow, and decision quality, organizations typically face three scenarios:

  • Weak evidence, poor flow, and low decision quality: First clarify the root cause: capability gap, lack of customer/data access, insufficient decision rights, unclear strategy, or poor collaboration. Then choose coaching, system support, scope adjustment, or role change when needed.
  • Outcome unclear, but discovery, flow, and decisions are improving: Continue investing, set learning goals, and build evidence quality before drawing conclusions about effectiveness.
  • Short-term outcomes look good but flow, quality, or sustainability decline: Adjust discovery, prioritization, or trade-off practices before scaling the investment.

From this angle, measurement is no longer about "scoring." It answers one question: should we continue trusting the current way of working?

An organization rarely lacks Product Owners.
It often lacks a system clear enough to recognize who is truly creating value.

Summary outline

  • The PO is accountable for maximizing product value in Scrum.
  • When the PO underperforms, teams typically respond three ways: passive execution, unstructured pushback, or compensating for the role.
  • All three responses carry long-term costs and do not address the root PO capability issue.
  • The real gap lies in organizational capability and operating mechanisms beyond Scrum.
  • PO training is necessary, but it must be paired with clear effectiveness measurement.
  • Avoid output-heavy indicators like task count, velocity, or requirement document detail.
  • Prefer value-oriented signals: Product Goal clarity, prioritization consistency, idea-to-impact time, and real usage feedback.
  • The practical PO framework has four observation layers: Outcome and Value, Discovery and Learning, Flow and Collaboration, and Decision Quality.
  • The Outcome layer asks whether the PO selects the right problems and creates user/business impact.
  • The Discovery and Learning layer asks whether the PO reduces uncertainty before large commitments.
  • The Flow and Collaboration layer asks whether the PO clears the path or creates bottlenecks for the team.
  • The Decision Quality and Enablement layer asks whether decisions are clear, consistent, and properly authorized.
  • The Product Owner Development Rubric distinguishes between Developing, Supported, Reliable, and Multiplying levels.
  • Minimum viable PO practice: Sprint Goal clarity, pre-refinement, post-release evidence agreement, and transparent priority changes.
  • From measurement to decision: based on signal combinations, organizations decide whether to invest further, adjust ways of working, or change role/scope.
  • Measurement must serve action-oriented decisions, not formal scoring.

Open questions for Product Owners

  • What should a Product Owner do when a stakeholder requests a specific solution, but the underlying user problem has not been clarified yet?
  • How can a Product Owner distinguish between a request that truly creates value and one driven mainly by personal opinion, internal pressure, or old habits?
  • When multiple stakeholders insist that their requests are top priority, what principles or data should a Product Owner use to set transparent priorities?
  • What should a Product Owner do when the Product Goal exists on paper but is not actually used to guide backlog decisions and day-to-day choices?
  • When the Product Goal changes, what should a Product Owner clarify so the Scrum Team understands it as necessary adaptation, not random direction switching?
  • How can a Product Owner prevent the Product Backlog from becoming a stakeholder request list instead of a tool to move toward the Product Goal?
  • How much detail is enough for Product Backlog Items so Developers can select work for a Sprint without turning refinement into a heavy analysis phase?
  • When Developers repeatedly ask for clarification on goals, scope, or acceptance criteria during a Sprint, should the Product Owner treat this as a backlog clarity issue, a product discovery issue, or a collaboration issue?
  • What should a Product Owner do when Developers challenge a requested solution as overly complex, high risk, or not the best way to solve the problem?
  • When Product Owner and Developers disagree on solution direction, who should decide what, and how can disagreement lead to a better decision rather than personal conflict?
  • What should a Product Owner do when a fast decision is needed but data is still incomplete? What should be assumed, what should be tested, and what should not be delayed?
  • When a released feature does not reach expected adoption, conversion, or impact, how should a Product Owner reassess assumptions, problem framing, solution choice, or measurement approach?
  • Which signals should a Product Owner use to evaluate outcome: adoption, activation, retention, conversion, support tickets, customer feedback, cost reduction, risk reduction, or other indicators?
  • How can a Product Owner avoid optimizing one metric while unintentionally harming user experience, product quality, or operational cost?
  • When one outcome improves but defects, support tickets, or maintenance cost rise sharply, how should a Product Owner balance short-term gains and long-term sustainability?
  • What should a Product Owner do to ensure Sprint Review is not just a demo of completed items, but a forum to validate assumptions, gather feedback, and adjust product direction?
  • When stakeholders do not attend Sprint Review or only focus on deadlines, how can a Product Owner create a more meaningful feedback loop?
  • What should a Product Owner do when stakeholders or managers send work directly to Developers, bypassing the Product Backlog and weakening agreed priorities?
  • When an urgent request appears mid-Sprint, how should a Product Owner evaluate its impact against the risk of breaking the Sprint Goal?
  • How can a Product Owner make trade-offs transparent when choosing among new features, technical debt, defect fixes, compliance, security, or user experience improvements?
  • When technical debt increasingly affects delivery, what should a Product Owner do to include this long-term cost in prioritization decisions instead of focusing only on new features?
  • What should a Product Owner do when team velocity is stable, backlog is always full, and many features are released, but there is still no clear evidence that the product is moving closer to the Product Goal?
  • How can a Product Owner tell the difference between "the team is delivering slowly" and "the team is dealing with an unclear backlog, constant change, or delayed decisions"?
  • When the organization evaluates Product Owners by release count, deadlines, or short-term stakeholder satisfaction, how should a Product Owner defend outcome-oriented thinking?
  • How should a Product Owner respond when people expect them to have all the answers, even though real product development requires shared discovery with Developers, stakeholders, and users?
  • When a Product Owner lacks authority over priority, budget, or product scope, is the issue the Product Owner's capability, or the organization's decision structure?
  • How should a Product Owner escalate when stakeholder conflicts cannot be resolved within the Scrum Team?
  • How can a Product Owner know they are truly maximizing product value, rather than simply becoming a more efficient backlog manager?
