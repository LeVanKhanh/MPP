1. Scrum Team - Product Owner (PO)

Core issue: what happens when the PO cannot provide clear value direction for the product.

Published on: 28/03/2026

Problem context: the PO role and Scrum expectations

Products rarely fail because they lack features. They fail when value remains unclear. According to the Scrum Guide, the PO is accountable for maximizing product value by understanding the Product Goal, market context, user needs, and priorities, then communicating those clearly to the development team.

However, a practical question must be asked: what happens when the PO does not meet these expectations? What if direction is unclear, priorities are inconsistent, or value is misidentified?

In that case, should the development team continue executing requests passively, or should it also take responsibility for clarifying and protecting product value?

Three common team responses when PO direction is weak

When a PO cannot provide clear value direction, Scrum does not offer a concrete prescription. In practice, teams usually respond in three ways, and each response has distinct consequences.

1) Passive execution. This is the most common response. The development team keeps accepting requests and operates like an "execution team," avoiding debate to preserve pace and stability. In the short term, sprints finish, velocity looks stable, and backlog items keep moving. In the long term, however, the product loses direction, value is not optimized, and team ownership fades.

2) Pushback without structure. Some teams challenge PO decisions when they see problems. This is a positive signal because the team starts caring about real product value. But if pushback is emotional, data-poor, or unsupported by a clear mechanism, conflict grows, collaboration declines, and decisions become slower and less consistent.

3) Compensating for the PO role. In more mature teams, developers do more than challenge. They actively clarify value by questioning goals, proposing solutions, and even reprioritizing work themselves. This may help in the short term, but over time it creates role drift: the team gradually carries PO responsibilities while the PO loses strategic direction ownership.

The gap that lies beyond Scrum

Scrum is not incomplete; it simply stops where it should. What comes next is an organizational capability question.

None of the three responses truly solves the root problem. Scrum exposes the gap, but filling that gap requires capabilities outside Scrum itself.

This leads to a deeper question: how can an organization develop PO capability, instead of forcing teams to constantly "adapt" to PO weaknesses?

From PO training to the measurement problem

If PO training works, that is valuable. Over time, the organization may have a PO who meets Scrum expectations.

But a more practical question remains: how do we know the training is truly effective? In other words, what evidence tells us a PO is actually meeting the role as intended?

Without a clear measurement system, evaluation usually falls back to perception: "the PO seems better," "the team feels smoother." These signals may be true, but they are not sufficient for decisions about continued investment, adjustment, or role change.

A common trap: measuring output instead of outcome

When organizations begin to measure PO effectiveness, they often fall into a familiar trap: measuring what is easy to see rather than what truly reflects value.

Scrum talks about "maximizing value" but does not define a fixed measurement model. Without care, measurement can drift off target from the beginning.

What should not be measured (or is often misread): number of backlog items written, requirement detail level, team velocity, and number of tasks completed in a sprint. Common issue: these indicators mostly measure output, not outcome.

Signals worth measuring and contextual interpretation

Better value-related signals include Product Goal clarity, consistency in prioritization, lead time from idea to impact (Idea -> Impact), and user or stakeholder feedback.

Still, these indicators must be interpreted in context, not applied as a rigid formula.

There is no single "Scrum-standard KPI set" for PO performance. Instead of chasing one perfect number, organizations should return to foundational questions: does the team understand why this work matters, is what gets built actually used, and is the product moving closer to the Product Goal?

Measuring PO Effectiveness

Measurement is not about "proving whether the PO is good enough." It is about enabling decisions: continue investing, adjust, or change.

If measurement ends at evaluation and does not lead to action, then measurement itself creates no value.

Scrum does not prescribe a value measurement method, and that gap forces organizations to confront a harder question: what do we actually mean by "value"?

Practical PO Measurement Framework

Principle

  • Do not measure how busy the PO is.
  • Measure whether the PO reduces uncertainty and creates value.

Framework structure (3 layers)

  1. Outcome: Are we building the right thing?
  2. Flow Efficiency: Is the PO slowing the team down?
  3. Decision Quality: Is the PO thinking clearly?

Layer 1 - Outcome (40%): Is the PO choosing high-value problems?

Metrics

  • Feature Adoption Rate: % of users actually using new features after release.
  • User Impact: e.g., retention, complaints, conversion.
  • Business Proxy: revenue, cost saving, usage frequency.

Perfect data is not required; trend and direction are what matter.

Scoring

  • Strong positive impact.
  • Neutral / unclear.
  • Negative / wasted.

Layer 2 - Flow Efficiency: Is the PO an enabler or a bottleneck?

Metrics

  1. Backlog Readiness Rate: % of items ready before sprint (clear AC, no ambiguity).
  2. Dev Clarification Interruptions: How often developers need clarification during the sprint. Low -> PO is clear. High -> PO is guessing / unclear.
  3. Rework Rate: % of work redone due to wrong requirements.
  4. Blocked Time Due to PO: Time spent waiting for PO decisions.

Scoring

  • Smooth flow.
  • Some friction.
  • Constant disruption.

Layer 3 - Decision Quality: Is the PO thinking like a product owner, or just managing the backlog?

Evaluate through observable behavior.

  1. Decision Latency: How long the PO takes to decide when needed. Fast when low risk. Careful when high impact.
  2. Consistency: Does the PO change direction frequently? High churn = weak thinking.
  3. Trade-off Thinking: Can the PO explain: Why this over that? What are we sacrificing?
  4. Problem Framing: Does the PO define the problem clearly, or jump straight to a solution?

Scoring

  • Clear, fast, logical.
  • Sometimes unclear.
  • Random / reactive.

From Measurement to Decision

Based on signals across outcome, flow efficiency, and decision quality, organizations usually face three situations:

  • The PO creates no meaningful outcome and also reduces team effectiveness: This is no longer a training issue. Consider changing role fit or responsibility scope.
  • The PO has no clear outcome yet, but team flow is healthy and decision quality is high: The issue may be strategy or context. Continue investing and supporting the PO.
  • The PO achieves some outcomes but hurts flow or causes high rework: This is a danger signal: short-term wins with long-term cost. Adjust the way of working before scaling.

From this angle, measurement is no longer about scoring people. It answers one question: should we keep trusting the current way of working?

Organizations rarely lack Product Owners.
What they often lack is a system clear enough to identify who is truly creating value.

Outline Summary Notes

  • In Scrum, the PO is accountable for maximizing product value.
  • When PO performance is weak, teams often respond in three ways: passive execution, unstructured pushback, or compensating for the PO role.
  • All three responses carry long-term costs and do not resolve the PO capability root cause.
  • The real gap is organizational capability and operating mechanisms beyond Scrum.
  • PO training matters, but it must be paired with clear effectiveness measurement.
  • Avoid output-heavy indicators such as task count, velocity, or requirement-document detail.
  • Prefer value-oriented signals: Product Goal clarity, prioritization consistency, idea-to-impact time, and real usage feedback.
  • The practical PO framework has three layers: Outcome (40%), Flow Efficiency, and Decision Quality.
  • The Outcome layer asks whether the PO selects the right problems and creates user/business impact.
  • The Flow Efficiency layer asks whether the PO enables flow or creates bottlenecks for development.
  • The Decision Quality layer asks whether the PO demonstrates product thinking, consistency, and trade-off reasoning.
  • From measurement to decision: based on signal combinations, organizations should decide whether to invest further, adjust ways of working, or change role/scope.
  • Measurement must support action-oriented decisions, not just formal scoring.
