2. Scrum Team - Scrum Master

Core issue: the Scrum Master is a change catalyst, but effectiveness depends simultaneously on personal capability and system conditions.

Published on: 04/04/2026

Setting the question: is the Scrum Master a coach or an organizer?

The Scrum Master is often described as a servant leader and a coach who helps the team move toward self-organization.

However, the important question is: where does the Scrum Master's actual influence lie, and how can the effectiveness of this role be evaluated?

The Scrum Guide describes what the Scrum Master should do, but it does not fully explain how to do it well within the realities of an organizational context.

Resistance from the team itself

The team is not always ready to listen to the Scrum Master. When coaching does not create clear value, it is easily perceived as theoretical or disconnected from practice.

The Scrum Master can provide direction, but the choice to act on it ultimately belongs to the team.

The question that follows is: does coaching actually drive change, or does it stop at the level of suggestion?

Power limits within an organizational environment

The Scrum Master is expected to protect the team from interruptions so it can focus on delivering Increments, but decisions from management can run counter to the spirit of Scrum.

In those moments, the Scrum Master faces a difficult choice: hold firm to coaching principles, or compromise in order to survive within the system.

This is not merely a framework problem; it is a problem of power and organizational structure.

Removing impediments: real or performative?

The remove-impediments role assumes the Scrum Master can both identify obstacles and act effectively to resolve them.

In practice, many impediments are rooted in structure, processes, or high-level decisions that fall outside the Scrum Master's direct control.

When proposals are not heard or not acted upon, the impediment-removal role easily becomes performative.

Misunderstanding the role and the phenomenon of fake agility

Scrum Masters rarely fail because of technical mistakes; they fail because the role is misunderstood from the start.

In many organizations, the Scrum Master is reduced to:

  • A meeting organizer.
  • A tool updater.
  • A progress tracker on behalf of the team.

The Scrum ceremonies remain intact, but the substance has changed; the team works the old way under a new layer of process.

The result is fake agility: Scrum events exist in form, but real Agility does not.

Measuring effectiveness and the sustainability of the Scrum Master role

Unlike the Product Owner, the Scrum Master's impact is harder to measure because it is typically indirect: better communication, smoother processes, problems surfaced earlier.

The Scrum Guide provides no standard KPIs, so effectiveness tends to be judged by gut feeling and is difficult to demonstrate.

When there is no clear measure, no direct authority, and a constant need to build influence, the Scrum Master risks burning out or retreating to the safe path: maintaining ceremonies instead of driving change.

Solution: empowerment is a necessary condition

In many organizations, a Scrum Team coexists with a Project Manager, an Engineering Manager, and a Scrum Master; real authority is usually concentrated in the management roles.

The Scrum Master needs sufficient space and authority to have impact, and must not be managed as a purely executional role.

Empowerment does not mean operating outside the organization; it means having support to drive meaningful change rather than only delivering formal recommendations.

Capability is a sufficient condition

Once the space exists, the next question is whether the Scrum Master has the capability to deliver value.

That capability must be developed through training, observation, and systematic evaluation tied to real team outcomes rather than surface-level activities.

Identifying fake agility through operational questions

  1. Does the team make its own decisions? Or does it still depend on top-down direction?
  2. Is the team focused on outcomes or outputs? Creating value, or just hitting metrics and closing tasks?
  3. Can the team operate steadily without external intervention? This is the true signal of maturity.

If the team remains dependent and operates in a performative mode, the problem does not lie with the team alone; it also reflects the limits of the Scrum Master's capability or scope of influence.

When the Scrum Master lacks sufficient authority

This is a common situation: expected to drive change while lacking the power to enforce it.

The typical approaches include:

  • Creating small wins to build trust.
  • Clarifying the trade-offs when the organization acts against Scrum principles.
  • Making problems and their impacts transparent.

The essence is to help the organization see the problem before it is forced to pay the price for it.

The Scrum Master does not just "point out problems"; they must make the cost of not changing visible and real.

If the Scrum Master has already made problems transparent, proposed solutions, and produced some results — but the organization still does not change — they must decide between continuing to seek a more suitable environment or accepting the current state.

Scrum Master capability framework (3 axes)

  1. Team self-organization: Does the team make autonomous decisions within its scope, and does it operate steadily when the Scrum Master steps back?
  2. Outcome over output: Does the team understand the Sprint Goal, measure real results, and dare to change direction when value is not being delivered?
  3. Continuous improvement: Do Retrospectives produce real change, are problems addressed at the root, and does the team's adaptation speed improve over time?

The principle: do not measure how busy the Scrum Master is; measure the change they create in the team and the system.

If the Scrum Master leaves and the team still operates better than before, that is a sign of success. If there is no difference, the role has not delivered real value.

Conclusion

The Scrum Master's effectiveness is the result of an interaction between personal capability and system conditions.

When either is missing, Scrum easily falls into a state that is formally correct but practically hollow.

In many cases, however, the Scrum Master is not the person who directly creates change — they are the person who reveals that the organization is not yet truly ready to change.

Therefore, the core question is not only:

"Is the Scrum Master doing a good job?"

but also:

"Is the system — and the people in it — genuinely ready to change, or are they simply running a ceremonial version of Agile?"

The Scrum Master is not a solution in themselves; they are a catalyst. And a catalyst only matters when the reaction is actually allowed to happen.

Summary outline

  • The Scrum Master is a coach and servant leader, but real-world impact depends heavily on organizational context.
  • Common limitations: team resistance, indirect authority, impediments beyond the Scrum Master's control.
  • Fake agility occurs when Scrum events run as scheduled but the team is neither self-organizing nor value-focused.
  • Empowerment is a necessary condition for the Scrum Master to have meaningful impact.
  • Capability is the sufficient condition for creating sustainable change.
  • Scrum Master measurement should be based on 3 axes: self-organization, outcome over output, and continuous improvement.
  • Do not measure surface activities; measure the real change in the team and the system over time.
  • If the Scrum Master leaves and the team still operates better than before, that is a sign the role built a lasting foundation.
  • When direct authority is absent, the Scrum Master must make the cost of not changing clear — not simply name the problem.
  • The closing question is not only whether the Scrum Master performed well, but whether the organization was truly ready to change or was merely performing Agile in form.
