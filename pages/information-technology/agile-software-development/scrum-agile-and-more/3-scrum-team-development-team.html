3. Scrum Team - Developers

Core issue: Developers are accountable for turning Product Backlog into valuable Increments, but capability, measurement, and system conditions determine sustained effectiveness.

In many organizations, Developers are still viewed as executors. In Scrum, that is a misunderstanding at the root.

If the Product Owner is accountable for deciding what deserves priority, and the Scrum Master helps Scrum be understood and practiced effectively, then Developers are where value becomes a real product.

Value does not exist in plans. Value does not live in process diagrams either. Value is only validated when a product is actually built, actually used, and actually creates impact.

And Developers are the ones who make that happen.

Not by simply "finishing tasks," but by continuously choosing the best way to turn ideas into real value.

A good plan is not yet value. A correct process is not yet value. Only when a product is truly built, used, and impactful can value be verified.

So Developers are not just "doers." They are where value is realized in product form - where product, technical, and delivery decisions are tested by real outcomes.

Problem framing

Issue 1: Developers lack baseline capability for real autonomy

Scrum expects Developers to have enough capability to turn Product Backlog items into valuable Increments autonomously. Most teams do not start there. They may lack skill depth or experience, yet still have to deliver continuously.

A group of Developers can only be truly autonomous when they can understand problems, make decisions, remove obstacles, and own outcomes. Without baseline capability, autonomy easily becomes a ceremonial expectation.

Highly autonomous Developers do not need daily task-level control. They need clear goals, full context, fitting decision rights, and support to remove blockers.

The paradox appears here: Developers are expected to be autonomous, but not fully invested in to become autonomous.

In theory, the right people naturally come together as a team. In practice, they are often assembled like functional pieces.

And when results fail expectations, organizations tend to measure output instead of what should have been built first: team capability itself.

At that point, autonomy is no longer an achievable goal. It becomes a formal expectation.

Issue 2: Measurement pressure

Scrum does not provide a fixed formula to measure Developer effectiveness - and that is not a flaw.

Scrum is not designed to maximize output mechanically. It is designed to create transparency, inspection, and adaptation loops that keep teams moving toward value.

In practice, however, organizations still need data for evaluation.

Without suitable measures, they often fall back to what is easiest to count: completed work volume.

As a result, Developers are pulled into a game where real value gets replaced by output.

Then teams no longer optimize for value. They optimize for what is easiest to measure.

Issue 3: Management intervention

Scrum creates a deliberate working rhythm where work is organized around one goal each Sprint.

But when urgent requests keep cutting in, that rhythm disappears.

This reflects a reality: the organization does not truly trust how Scrum handles change.

Instead of accepting controlled feedback cycles, the organization intervenes immediately - and unintentionally breaks the very mechanism that enables effective Developer performance.

This is also a sign of a deeper problem: when vision or planning capability is weak, everything can become "urgent."

Developers stop working by goal and start reacting to change.

When everything becomes a priority, no priority truly exists.

This does not mean all management intervention is wrong. In some contexts, new information, operational risks, security or legal issues, or major business-direction shifts may require adjustment.

The issue is not whether change happens, but how change enters the system. If intervention goes through transparent inspection-and-adaptation mechanisms, Scrum can absorb it. If change appears as sudden command, it breaks the Sprint, weakens autonomy, and erodes Developers' ability to focus on goals.

The capability erosion loop

These three issues are not isolated incidents. They are a chain reaction.

Each cycle pushes Developers farther from their original ability to create value.

When team capability is weak, organizations try to control through what is easy to measure - output.

When output becomes the main metric, pressure increases while capability does not.

When results still fail expectations, intervention appears and breaks the rhythm that would have helped the team improve.

A loop forms:

capability gaps -> wrong measurement -> intervention -> weaker improvement ability -> deeper capability gaps.

Scrum is still performed. Ceremonies still happen. But the internal system no longer operates the way Scrum is designed to work.

A system can look very busy and still fail to create value.

Solutions

Principle 1: Build capability as a system responsibility, not an individual burden

A team's ability to operate autonomously is not a preexisting state. It is the result of deliberate investment over time.

In reality, Developers are rarely formed under ideal conditions. So instead of waiting for "the right people," organizations should work with the team they have.

That responsibility does not sit with Developers alone. It sits in how the organization designs their working environment.

Capability observation and assessment are necessary, but the goal is not judgment. The goal is to identify gaps that need support.

Then training, mentoring, pair work, hiring, or team redesign are no longer temporary fixes. They become part of how the system runs.

Principle 2: Make value the center of every decision

When value is not clearly defined, the system defaults to what is easiest to measure: output volume.

Then "doing more" is easily mistaken for "creating more value."

So the key question is not whether Developers are doing enough. It is whether they are doing what truly matters.

This requires decision-makers to understand how value is created in Scrum: not through paper plans, but through validated real-world outcomes.

When value becomes central, supporting Developers no longer depends on one manager. It becomes a shared system responsibility.

Then the team is no longer dragged by output pressure, but guided by the meaning of what they build.

Principle 3: Design measurement to shape the right behavior

How an organization measures determines how teams behave. [3]

In other words, measurement does not just reflect behavior. It actively creates behavior.

When performance is measured by completed work count, Developers optimize for more completion, not more value.

So the measurement system should not focus on "How much did Developers do?" It should focus on "Did what was built actually create value?"

This does not mean removing all output metrics. It means putting them in the right place: supporting signals, not primary goals.

This is also the foundation for a KPI system that does not distort Developer behavior.

When these three principles are designed together, the system starts to change.

When capability is built deliberately, value is centered, and measurement drives healthy behavior, the earlier loop starts to break.

Developers are no longer trapped between pressure and intervention. They can focus on what matters most: creating real value.

At that point, Scrum is no longer a process to "follow." It becomes a natural system for creating value.

Developer effectiveness observation system

Organizations need to observe Developer effectiveness, but should not seek one simple score to decide who is good and who is not.

In Scrum, an Increment is a collaborative result. [1] Change quality does not come from lines of code, closed tickets, or pull request volume alone. It depends on problem clarity, technical decision quality, collaboration with Product Owner, system stability, cross-team dependencies, and the working conditions created by the organization.

So the goal of an observation system is not to answer:

Which Developer works the most?

It is to answer:

Do Developers have the conditions to create value in a stable, autonomous, and sustainable way?

This system should help organizations surface key questions early:

  • Which skills does the team still lack to complete end-to-end work?
  • Is system technical quality improving or degrading?
  • Do Developers understand the right problem before building?
  • Where is work getting stuck, and why?
  • Is the team learning, reducing risk, and improving over time?

Metrics should be used to drive dialogue and improvement action. They should not be used as personal reward-penalty KPIs, cross-team ranking tools, or context-free replacements for professional judgment.

Design principles for the observation system

A healthy measurement system should follow five principles.

1. Observe at team level first

Developer effectiveness is primarily system effectiveness in collaboration. Individual assessment should serve capability development, mentoring, role fit, or career growth, not be inferred directly from team delivery metrics.

2. Use trends, not one-time snapshots

One Sprint with more bugs or higher cycle time is not automatically a problem. The key is trend across multiple Sprints: is quality consistently declining, are dependencies increasing, is rework recurring?

3. Combine quantitative and qualitative evidence

Data shows what is happening. Conversations with Developers, Sprint Review, Retrospective, technical review, and user feedback explain why.

4. Every signal should lead to actionable response

A metric that leads to no decision, experiment, or improvement only adds reporting. For example, if rework rises because requirements are unclear, the response is not to ask Developers to move faster, but to improve refinement and problem validation with Product Owner.

5. Do not optimize output metrics as primary goals

Velocity, ticket count, pull requests, and working hours can be useful reference data. But when they become primary targets, they distort behavior and push Developers toward "doing more" instead of "creating better value."

Developer effectiveness framework

Developer effectiveness can be observed through five dimensions: Capability, Technical Sustainability, Value Collaboration, Flow Reliability, and Ownership and Improvement. This keeps the view multidimensional instead of reducing productivity to one number. [2]

These five dimensions should not be collapsed into one "performance score." They should be shown as a team capability and health profile.

1. Capability

Capability is not just coding skill. It is the combined ability to turn a Product Backlog Item into a usable, quality Increment that can operate in reality.

A capable team does not need an expert in every specialty. But it needs enough skill coverage to avoid excessive dependency on one person, one manager, or one external team.

Signals to observe

MetricHow to ObserveWarning Sign
Skills coverageBuild a skills matrix across domain, coding, testing, architecture, DevOps, security, data, and incident handlingA critical skill is held by one person only, or missing entirely
Key-person dependencyObserve how often items are delayed or blocked waiting for one specific personThe team cannot continue when one person is away, overloaded, or transferred
End-to-end delivery capabilityRate of backlog items completed fully under Definition of Done without unusual external rescueDevelopers can code but cannot test, deploy, operate, or validate outcomes
Problem-solving maturityObserve how the team analyzes causes, proposes options, and handles incidentsTeam waits for detailed instructions instead of proposing approaches

LevelDescription
1 - Highly dependentThe team frequently needs detailed direction and relies on specific people or external teams for basic completion.
2 - Can deliver with supportThe team completes most work but still depends significantly on seniors, managers, or external specialists.
3 - Autonomous in normal conditionsThe team can handle most end-to-end work, knows when to escalate, and collaborates effectively.
4 - Autonomous and expanding capabilityThe team not only self-delivers, but also reduces dependency, shares knowledge, and raises collective capability.

This score is not for judging people. It helps answer: where do we need mentoring, training, pair work, hiring, or structural redesign?

2. Technical Sustainability

Developers create value not only by releasing features, but by keeping systems simple enough, stable enough, secure enough, and changeable enough for the future.

A feature that works today but sharply increases complexity, defects, or maintenance cost may create short-term value while damaging long-term delivery capability.

MetricHow to ObserveWarning Sign
Escaped defectsDefects found after release, categorized by severity and causeProduction defects keep increasing or the same defect type repeats
Defect recurrence rateRate of repeated defects caused by unresolved root causesTeam fixes symptoms but not system causes
Change failure rateRate of releases causing rollback, hotfix, incidents, or serious impactEvery change introduces high operational risk
Technical debt trendVolume and severity of technical debt recorded, resolved, or accumulatingTechnical debt rises steadily without capacity to address it
Maintainability trendTrend of complexity, meaningful code smells, coupling, testability, and effort needed for changeSmall changes take longer, trigger chain effects, or are hard to test
Operational stabilityIncident trend, recovery time, alert quality, and system observabilityTeam learns about issues only after user complaints

Do not set absolute goals such as "zero bugs" or "zero code smells." Real systems always include defects, debt, and trade-offs. Observe trend instead: is the system becoming more stable or less stable after each change, are root causes being handled, and is change cost increasing over time?

3. Value Collaboration

Product Owner is accountable for maximizing product value and setting priority. But Developers are not there to just receive and implement requests.

Developers are accountable for helping Product Owner clarify problems, challenge solutions, surface technical risks, and propose ways to reach goals with better cost-complexity-risk trade-offs.

MetricHow to ObserveWarning Sign
Refinement readinessRate of backlog items entering Sprint while still missing goals, acceptance criteria, data, business decisions, or technical prerequisitesSprint starts before Developers truly understand the problem
Rework due to misunderstandingRate of rework from misunderstood requirements, missing context, or late confirmationRework appears repeatedly after development is nearly complete
Solution alternatives proposedCases where Developers proactively propose simpler, lower-risk, or earlier-validatable optionsDevelopers only execute the initial description despite seeing poor fit
Early validation rateRate of items validated early via prototype, spike, feature flag, staged rollout, or small releasesTeam builds too much before knowing whether a solution fits
Post-release impact reviewRate of key items reviewed after release against expected outcomesRelease equals finish, with no evidence of value

During refinement, Developers and Product Owner should answer together: what user problem exists, what outcome is desired, what smaller test could validate assumptions, what technical or operational risks exist, and how success will be confirmed after release.

An effective Developer team does not only build correctly. It helps the organization build the right thing.

4. Flow Reliability

Flow is not pressure to make Developers move faster. Flow helps the organization see where work is blocked, why delivery time increases, and which conditions are eroding focus.

MetricHow to ObserveWarning Sign
Sprint Goal reliabilityRate of Sprint Goals achieved, not just ticket completion rateMany tickets completed but Sprint Goal missed
Spillover trendRate of items repeatedly moving to later SprintsOversized work, weak refinement, high dependency, or constant scope shifts
Blocked timeTotal blocked waiting time by cause categoryWaiting for decisions, environment, API, reviews, or other teams
Unplanned work ratioShare of unplanned work vs planned Sprint capacityIncidents, manager requests, or urgent tasks repeatedly break Sprint Goal
Rework ratioEffort spent redoing work due to requirement changes, misunderstanding, defects, or weak designTeam spends most capacity on rework
Work item agingCount of items staying too long in In Progress or BlockedWIP too high or poor slicing
Dependency loadNumber of external dependencies directly affecting Sprint GoalTeam lacks control over its own delivery

When flow worsens, organizations should not ask, "How can Developers go faster?" The better question is: "What in the system is slowing Developers down, interrupting them, or preventing sustainable completion?" [4]

For example, rising spillover may require better refinement and slicing; rising blocked time may require better decision paths; rising unplanned work may require investment in quality, observability, or reserve capacity; rising dependencies may require boundary, ownership, or technical-roadmap changes.

5. Ownership and Improvement

Capability, technical quality, and flow are only sustainable when Developers own collective outcomes.

Ownership does not mean carrying every issue alone. It means not stopping at assigned tasks. Developers surface risks, bring problems into the open, propose responses, and improve the system together.

MetricHow to ObserveWarning Sign
Improvement action completionRate of Retrospective actions implemented and validatedRetrospectives repeat but produce no real change
Problem recurrenceCount of repeated issues across Sprints or incidentsTeam treats incidents one by one without root-cause learning
Risk visibilityRate of risks raised early before becoming blockers or incidentsRisks are reported only after delivery impact occurs
Knowledge sharingPair work, technical sessions, documentation, quality review, and cross-trainingKnowledge remains in individual heads
Proactive improvementImprovement proposals for tooling, architecture, test automation, monitoring, or process that are tested in practiceTeam reacts only to external requests and does not improve system proactively

Ownership and Improvement observation scale

LevelDescription
1 - Reactive behaviorTeam mainly handles assigned work and reports issues only after serious impact.
2 - Problem awarenessTeam sees issues but lacks mechanisms or capability to resolve them fully.
3 - Proactive improvementTeam raises risks early, executes actions, and learns from outcomes.
4 - Improvement as system capabilityTeam improves internally and shares effective practices, reduces dependency, and helps other teams grow.

Metrics that should not become performance KPIs

Some metrics are still useful, but only as supporting data:

  • Velocity.
  • Ticket completion count.
  • Pull request count.
  • Lines of code.
  • Individual Developer cycle time.
  • Working hours.
  • Bug count tied to one person.
  • Comment volume or number of meetings attended.

These are easy to count but do not fully represent value, complexity, risk, or quality.

When used for reward-penalty or individual ranking, Developers start optimizing numbers: splitting tickets artificially, choosing easy tasks, avoiding complex responsibility, creating formal PR volume, or hiding risks to protect metrics.

At that point, measurement no longer reflects effectiveness. It starts producing ineffective behavior.

Conclusion

Effective Developers are not those who do the most tasks, close the most tickets, create the most pull requests, or produce the highest velocity.

Effective Developers are those with enough capability, autonomy, collaboration ability, and ownership to turn Product Backlog into valuable Increments.

They do not just write code. They help understand problems, challenge solutions, control technical risk, keep systems stable, and preserve the product's ability to keep creating value over time.

So when evaluating Developer effectiveness, the key is not finding one number to control people. The key is observing whether the system enables Developers to perform their role well.

If Developers lack capability, the organization should invest in capability.

If Developers lack autonomy, the organization should revisit decision rights and working-environment design.

If value is replaced by output, the organization should redesign measurement.

If flow is broken by intervention and dependency, the organization should fix systemic causes instead of adding pressure on Developers.

This article does not go deep into Definition of Done, Sprint Goal, or Product Goal, because those are specific mechanisms in Scrum flow. The focus here is foundational: do Developers have enough capability and autonomy for those mechanisms to work effectively?

When capability, autonomy, value, and flow are observed properly, Scrum is no longer only a sequence of ceremonies. It becomes a system that helps Developers create sustainable value.

Illustrative example: when spillover is not just a speed problem

A team keeps carrying work into the next Sprint. A shallow response would be to ask Developers to estimate more carefully or increase pressure to finish. When viewed through this framework, the team finds three facts: Product Backlog items often enter the Sprint before business decisions are fully made; many items get blocked while waiting for another team's API; and an older set of defects consumes a meaningful share of capacity.

Signal Hypothesis Appropriate response Owner with authority to act
Spillover increases Items are too large or refinement is not sufficient Slice smaller, clarify acceptance, and confirm dependency before Sprint Planning PO + Developers
Blocked time increases Dependency lacks a clear commitment mechanism Escalate ownership, agree on interface contract, and define response timing Scrum Master + Manager / System Owner
Rework increases Old defects and missing test automation are consuming capacity Reserve capacity for root-cause fixes, automation, and observability Developers + Manager

In this case, spillover is not used to conclude that the team is simply “slow.” It is a signal that reveals backlog quality, dependency, and technical sustainability issues that Developers alone cannot fully solve.

Summary outline

  • Developers are not just executors; they are where value is realized as usable Increments.
  • Three common core problems are: weak baseline capability for real autonomy, measurement pressure that favors output, and management intervention that breaks Sprint rhythm.
  • These problems form a decline loop: weak capability → wrong measurement → intervention → weaker improvement ability → deeper capability gaps.
  • The solution must be designed at the system level: invest in capability, center value, and use measurement to shape the right behavior.
  • The Developer effectiveness observation system should include five dimensions: Capability, Technical Sustainability, Value Collaboration, Flow Reliability, and Ownership and Improvement.
  • Measurement should focus on trends, combine quantitative and qualitative evidence, and each signal should lead to concrete action.
  • Output metrics such as velocity, ticket count, or pull requests are only supporting data and should not become individual reward-penalty KPIs.
  • The final goal is not to control Developers with one number, but to create conditions for stable, autonomous, and sustainable value creation.

Open questions for Developers

  • When a requirement is unclear, what will Developers do to clarify it before starting: ask for more context, confirm acceptance criteria, propose a spike, or state a testable assumption?
  • What should Developers do when they see that a Product Owner or stakeholder requested solution is too complex, too risky, or not the most effective way to reach the intended outcome?
  • How can Developers challenge a solution while still respecting the Product Owner, stakeholders, and product decisions?
  • When Developers disagree with the Product Owner about the approach, is the disagreement a product decision, a technical decision, or something that should be broken down so both sides can resolve it together?
  • Are Developers proactively proposing a smaller, simpler, or earlier-testable solution before committing to a large scope?
  • When a Product Backlog Item enters a Sprint but still has significant uncertainty, what should Developers do to make the risks visible instead of silently accepting the work and hoping it can be handled?
  • How should Developers respond when the Sprint Goal is threatened: adjust the Sprint Backlog themselves, reduce scope, increase collaboration, or escalate a blocker that requires an external decision?
  • When a piece of work takes longer than expected, what should Developers do to distinguish between real complexity, incomplete refinement, dependency, technical debt, or an unsuitable way of slicing the work?
  • Are Developers optimizing for completing their own tickets, or supporting one another to achieve the Sprint Goal of the whole team?
  • When a team member is blocked or lacks capability in an important area, what should Developers do to support that person without creating a long-term dependency?
  • How can Developers reduce key-person risk when only one person understands or can safely change a specific area of the system?
  • What should Developers do when knowledge sharing keeps getting delayed because of short-term delivery pressure?
  • When code review becomes a bottleneck, what should Developers inspect: pull request size, review quality, ownership, lack of time, or missing technical standards?
  • What should Developers do when code review focuses only on style or small defects but ignores design, maintainability, security, testability, and technical trade-offs?
  • When defects or incidents repeat, what should Developers do to find the root cause instead of only fixing symptoms and closing the ticket?
  • How can Developers tell whether a defect is a local mistake, a sign of technical debt, or a system issue in Definition of Done, testing, or observability?
  • Are Developers being transparent with the Product Owner about technical debt, complexity, and maintenance cost, or are they accepting more technical debt to preserve short-term delivery speed?
  • When the Product Owner keeps prioritizing new features, what should Developers do to explain the trade-off between short-term delivery, quality, risk, and the product's future development capability?
  • What evidence should Developers provide so technical debt does not become a vague concept or a generic excuse for delaying delivery?
  • When a change is technically released successfully, how do Developers know whether the solution actually works in the real environment?
  • Do Developers participate in observing adoption, error rate, support tickets, performance, reliability, or user feedback after release?
  • When the outcome is not as expected, what should Developers review: the original assumption, solution fit, implementation quality, observability, or the way the release was tested?
  • What should Developers do when the environment, CI/CD, test automation, or monitoring is reducing the team's delivery capability?
  • When dependency on another team keeps blocking work items, what should Developers do to make the technical dependency, ownership, interface contract, and Sprint Goal impact visible?
  • How should Developers react when a Manager or stakeholder injects work directly into the middle of a Sprint, bypassing the Product Owner or Sprint Goal?
  • When unplanned work grows too much, are Developers treating it only as "unexpected work that must be done," or are they working with the Scrum Master and Product Owner to find the system cause that makes everything urgent?
  • How can Developers preserve autonomy without turning autonomy into "everyone just does their own thing" or a lack of transparency to the team?
  • What should Developers do when the team needs a technical decision but does not have enough data, time, or expertise to decide confidently?
  • When should Developers decide within their own expertise, when should they consult the Product Owner, and when should they escalate to a Manager or architect with decision authority?
  • Do Developers speak up when a Sprint commitment is no longer realistic, or do they stay silent to avoid making the team look like it is failing?
  • When the Sprint Goal is not achieved, do Developers focus on finding who slowed things down, or do they review how work selection, dependency, scope, quality, and system conditions were handled?
  • What should Developers do when Retrospectives keep raising the same issue but no action is completed or no real change happens?
  • How can Developers turn Retrospectives from a discussion about feelings into a space for small improvement experiments with an owner, validation criteria, and a review date?
  • Are Developers growing the capability of the whole team, or only improving their own capability and optimizing their personal work scope?
  • When a Developer produces a lot of code, many pull requests, or closes many tickets, in what context should the team evaluate that to avoid confusing output with value?
  • What should Developers do to ensure technical quality, maintainability, and the system's ability to change are not sacrificed for a short-term deadline?
  • How can Developers know whether the team is truly creating a valuable Increment, instead of only creating a lot of changes that look finished?
