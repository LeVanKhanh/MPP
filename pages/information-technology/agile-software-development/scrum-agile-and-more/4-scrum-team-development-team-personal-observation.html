4. Developers - Individual Capability Observation

Core issue: evaluate and develop individual capability without breaking Scrum Team collaboration.

Problem framing

No Development Team is perfect. A team is formed by individuals with different capability, experience, motivation, communication style, and commitment level. So helping members collaborate smoothly, trust one another, and move toward one shared goal is difficult. It does not happen automatically just because people are placed on one team.

The Scrum Guide expects Developers to self-manage: organize their own work, decide how to execute it, and share accountability for Sprint Goal. But self-management does not mean a team will always operate well under all conditions.

What happens when one or more people are not aligned with the team's working model: lacking required capability, not collaborating, not honoring commitments, withholding information, or repeatedly creating conflict? Can the team identify and correct this by itself?

The practical issue is not only asking teams to "self-organize." It is building mechanisms to detect misalignment early: dependency concentrated on one person, unstable work quality, commitments not met, unresolved conflict, unshared knowledge, or repeated load transfer onto a few members.

Then the key questions become: who should intervene, at what level, and how can personal issues be handled without damaging overall Development Team effectiveness?

How should individual capability be measured?

Individual capability measurement is not simple, yet every organization eventually needs to address it. It is also where tension with Scrum often appears.

Scrum emphasizes collaboration, shared accountability, and Scrum Team outcomes. A Sprint success or failure should not be attributed to one individual's credit or fault. This is necessary because products are created through combined roles, combined skills, and interdependent decisions.

However, that does not mean individual capability is unimportant.

A team does not exist independently from its members. Development Team quality is directly shaped by each person's professional capability, problem-solving ability, accountability, collaboration quality, and initiative. When some members repeatedly carry difficult work, solve critical issues, or help others through blockers, those contributions should be recognized. Conversely, when someone does not meet capability, commitment, or collaboration expectations, organizations should detect that early and support or adjust.

The real question is not whether to measure individual capability. The real question is how to measure it without breaking Scrum collaboration spirit.

If measurement relies only on ticket count, story points, lines of code, or bug volume handled, organizations easily create local optimization behavior: choosing easy work, splitting work artificially, avoiding shared responsibility, or reducing support for teammates.

So individual capability evaluation should include broader dimensions:

  • Ability to complete work with suitable quality and independence.
  • Ability to handle complexity, risk, and uncertainty.
  • Ability to collaborate, share knowledge, and raise collective team capability.
  • Accountability for shared outcomes, not just assigned tasks.
  • Ability to learn, absorb feedback, and improve over time.
  • Individual impact on product quality, codebase sustainability, and delivery effectiveness.

A healthy Scrum model should not choose between "team evaluation" and "individual evaluation." Organizations need both: evaluate collective outcomes to protect collaboration, and evaluate individual capability to develop people, detect capability gaps, and strengthen the Development Team over time.

The key question is: how can organizations evaluate individual contribution accurately without turning Scrum Team into internal competitors chasing metrics?

Individual capability framework in Development Team

1. Usage principles

This framework is not for ranking who closes more tickets. It is meant to answer four questions:

  1. At what level can this person create value?
  2. How independently can this person work?
  3. Does this person raise team capability and effectiveness?
  4. What should this person develop to handle broader responsibility?

Each evaluation should be grounded in concrete examples from the period, not general impressions or one isolated event.

Six core capability groups

A. Delivery Quality & Execution

Ability to complete work with suitable quality and independence

What to observe

  • Can they meet commitments on time at a reasonable quality level?
  • Do they clarify requirements proactively before starting?
  • Are code, tests, documentation, and handover quality stable?
  • Is their work frequently reworked, rolled back, or corrected by others?
  • Do they rely too heavily on seniors or managers to continue?

Suggested evidence

  • Pull requests and code review feedback.
  • Rework rate or post-release bugs.
  • Ability to complete tasks end to end.
  • Support needed for familiar work.
  • Quality of acceptance criteria, test cases, or technical documentation.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Requires close support
Can complete only very clear tasks, frequently needs guidance, misses edge cases, and has unstable quality.

Level 2 - Can handle familiar work
Can complete common tasks with periodic support; baseline quality is acceptable but still needs close review.

Level 3 - Independent and reliable
Can handle most work in role scope independently; clarifies requirements, manages progress, and maintains stable quality.

Level 4 - Leads delivery at broader scope
Can coordinate a feature or workstream, handle dependency and risk proactively, and help others complete work.

Level 5 - Raises team delivery standard
Designs or improves working methods that increase quality, reduce rework, reduce lead time, or improve release reliability for the team.

B. Problem Solving & Technical Judgment

Ability to handle complexity, risk, and ambiguity

What to observe

  • How do they respond when requirements are unclear?
  • Do they investigate root causes or only treat symptoms?
  • Can they reason about trade-offs among speed, quality, cost, and risk?
  • Do they know when to escalate or involve additional expertise?
  • Are technical decisions based on evidence and context?

Suggested evidence

  • Solution designs, ADRs, technical proposals.
  • Incident analysis or root-cause analysis.
  • Production issue handling quality.
  • Question quality during refinement or planning.
  • Technical decisions that reduced risk or complexity.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Solves only with instruction
Struggles when documentation is weak or multiple options exist; usually waits for others to decide.

Level 2 - Handles bounded problems
Can debug familiar issues but is still weak at root-cause depth and trade-off reasoning.

Level 3 - Analyzes problems independently
Can break down problems, identify causes, evaluate options, and propose sensible solutions.

Level 4 - Handles complex cross-cutting problems
Can resolve issues spanning multiple systems, stakeholders, or high-risk conditions and makes trade-offs explicit.

Level 5 - Builds organizational problem-solving capability
Shapes architecture principles or methods that help teams solve similar problem classes in the future.

C. Collaboration & Team Enablement

Ability to collaborate, share knowledge, and lift collective team capability

What to observe

  • Do they share information in time or keep knowledge siloed?
  • Do they help teammates with blockers?
  • Do they provide constructive review feedback?
  • Can they collaborate effectively with PO, QA, DevOps, managers, and other teams?
  • Do they help raise others' capability or only optimize their own tasks?

Suggested evidence

  • Code review quality.
  • Pair programming, mentoring, knowledge-sharing sessions.
  • Feedback from peers and adjacent roles.
  • Reduction in dependency on one individual.
  • Contribution to documentation, onboarding, playbooks, or reusable components.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Reactive collaboration
Mostly focused on own tasks, shares little, and often creates dependency for others.

Level 2 - Collaborates when asked
Coordinates at basic level but is not consistently proactive in helping others.

Level 3 - Reliable team member
Communicates clearly, supports teammates, shares knowledge, and protects team commitments.

Level 4 - Connector and capability multiplier
Mentors proactively, reduces silos, improves cross-role collaboration, and helps others become more independent.

Level 5 - Ecosystem-level enabler
Builds communities of practice, knowledge-sharing standards, or mechanisms that help multiple teams grow.

D. Ownership & Product Mindset

Accountability for shared outcomes, not only assigned tasks

What to observe

  • Do they only "close tickets," or care whether the feature actually works in use?
  • Do they raise risks, dependencies, or scope ambiguity early?
  • Do they ask about user value and product goals?
  • Do they participate in post-release issue handling?
  • Do they act on problems outside their assigned scope when team outcomes are at risk?

Suggested evidence

  • Behavior around blockers and production incidents.
  • Rate of early issue detection vs late detection.
  • Participation in Sprint Review, refinement, and retrospective.
  • Proactive improvements beyond assigned tickets.
  • Feedback from PO, QA, Support, or stakeholders.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Narrow ownership
Focuses only on assigned tasks and easily misses impact on shared goals.

Level 2 - Task-level responsibility
Completes assigned work but is not proactive about dependency or outcomes.

Level 3 - Scope-level ownership
Clarifies early, flags risks, and follows outcomes after delivery in owned scope.

Level 4 - Feature or Sprint Goal ownership
Coordinates across parties, resolves cross-role issues, and protects delivery goals.

Level 5 - Product or system-level ownership
Influences direction, prioritization, and decision quality at broader organizational level.

E. Learning Agility & Growth

Ability to learn, absorb feedback, and improve over time

What to observe

  • Do they receive feedback without defensiveness?
  • Do they turn feedback into concrete action?
  • Do they learn only for awareness, or also for application?
  • Do they develop skills aligned to team and product needs?
  • Is improvement visible over quarters or key periods?

Suggested evidence

  • Personal growth goals and actual outcomes.
  • New skills applied in delivery work.
  • Improvement after feedback, incidents, or retrospectives.
  • Ability to take on new scope or role.
  • Peer feedback on growth over time.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Passive learning
Learns only when asked and shows limited behavior change after feedback.

Level 2 - Individual growth effort
Shows learning and improvement but weak alignment with work needs.

Level 3 - Learns fast and applies
Turns feedback and new knowledge into practical work improvement.

Level 4 - Self-directed capability growth
Identifies skill gaps, creates learning plans, and applies them to harder problems.

Level 5 - Creates a learning environment for others
Turns personal lessons into documentation, mentoring, frameworks, or shared team practices.

F. Sustainable Impact

Impact on product quality, codebase sustainability, and delivery effectiveness

What to observe

  • Does their work create lasting impact or only short-term fixes?
  • Do they reduce technical debt, defects, incidents, rework, or dependency?
  • Do they create reusable assets, automation, or process improvements?
  • Do they improve observability, maintainability, testability, or security?
  • Do they help the team deliver faster without sacrificing quality?

Suggested evidence

  • Reduction in defect leakage or production incidents.
  • Reduced issue resolution, build, deploy, or cycle times.
  • Improved meaningful test coverage, observability, or automation.
  • Reusable assets: libraries, templates, pipelines, guidelines, components.
  • Improvements that reduce key-person risk or maintenance cost.

1-5 scale

Level 1 - Mostly short-term impact
Completes tasks but gives little attention to maintainability or long-term effects.

Level 2 - Some quality awareness
Follows existing standards but creates limited meaningful improvement.

Level 3 - Observable positive impact
Improves quality or delivery effectiveness in feature/module/team scope.

Level 4 - System-level improvement
Addresses root causes, reduces operational cost, or improves scalability.

Level 5 - Long-term leverage for multiple teams/products
Creates improvements that scale, set new standards, or materially raise organizational delivery capability.

Recommended scoring approach

Do not calculate scores mechanically. Weights can be used to guide discussion:

Capability group Suggested weight
Delivery Quality & Execution20%
Problem Solving & Technical Judgment20%
Collaboration & Team Enablement15%
Ownership & Product Mindset15%
Learning Agility & Growth10%
Sustainable Impact20%

Total score should remain a secondary indicator. The primary conclusion should be based on:

  • Clear strengths.
  • Most important capability gaps.
  • Fit with current level expectations.
  • Concrete work evidence.
  • Development goals for the next 3-6 months.

Evaluation mechanisms to reduce bias

1. Evidence-based evaluation

Each dimension should include at least 2-3 concrete examples: PRs, features, incidents, designs, peer feedback, delivery metrics, or mentoring activity.

2. Do not evaluate by output alone

Do not use ticket count, story points, commits, lines of code, or working hours as individual KPIs.

3. Calibrate across managers or technical leaders

The same "Level 3" should mean the same thing across teams. Without calibration, each manager applies a private standard.

4. Separate performance and potential evaluation

Someone may deliver strongly in current scope but not be ready for broader scope yet. Another person may be growing fast but has had limited opportunities to demonstrate it.

5. Evaluate by trend, not one Sprint

Use at least one quarter or 2-3 representative Sprints, especially for roles operating in complex systems.
