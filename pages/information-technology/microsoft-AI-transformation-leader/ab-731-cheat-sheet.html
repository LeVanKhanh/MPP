AB-731 — Consolidated Cheat Sheet

Microsoft AI Transformation Leader — learn in the order: business need → AI approach → Microsoft solution → governance → adoption → measurement

Consolidated from the prior cheat sheet and test notes. Updated: June 29, 2026

How to use this sheet

For every scenario, ask six questions in sequence:

  1. Business outcome: What decision, productivity gain, risk reduction, or customer outcome matters?
  2. AI pattern: Generative, predictive, conversational, document, vision, speech, search/RAG, or agent workflow?
  3. Data: Public web, user-provided content, Microsoft 365 work data, or external enterprise data?
  4. Microsoft product: Copilot Chat, Microsoft 365 Copilot, Copilot Studio, Foundry, Foundry Tools, Azure AI Search, Purview, or Security Copilot?
  5. Control: Permissions, privacy, security, Responsible AI, human oversight, evaluation, and cost?
  6. Adoption: Pilot, champions, workflow change, training, business KPI, then scale?

Exam anti-pattern: “Deploy widely first; governance, training, security, and measurement later” is normally the wrong choice.

2. AI fundamentals and business value

AI typeBest atTypical signal
Generative AICreate or transform text, code, images, summaries, drafts, explanations, and answers.“Draft, summarize, generate, rewrite, ask questions of content.”
Predictive AI / machine learningPredict or classify using patterns in historical data.“Forecast demand, detect fraud, predict churn, classify tickets.”
Conversational AINatural-language interaction for support, guidance, and task completion.“Chatbot, assistant, self-service support.”
Agentic AIReason across steps, retrieve information, invoke tools/actions, and complete a bounded workflow.“Follow approval steps, update a system, orchestrate work.”

When generative AI creates value

  • Scale: assist many users or process high volumes of similar requests.
  • Automation: reduce repetitive drafting, summarizing, classifying, extracting, or routing work.
  • Augmentation: improve speed and quality of human decisions, not necessarily replace people.
  • Knowledge access: ground answers in governed, current enterprise knowledge.

ROI logic: establish a baseline → estimate time, quality, revenue, risk, or service improvement → account for license, token, integration, data preparation, governance, training, and ongoing operations cost → validate in a pilot.

Core challenges and correct response

ChallengeMeaningPrimary response
Fabrication / hallucinationAI produces unsupported or incorrect content.Grounding, citations, evaluation, human review, and clear user guidance.
BiasUnequal or systematically unfair outcomes caused by data, design, or use.Representative data, fairness evaluation, monitoring, and human escalation.
ReliabilityOutput can vary or fail under different inputs and conditions.Test representative scenarios, add fallbacks, monitor production, and set boundaries.
Prompt injectionUntrusted content attempts to override instructions or misuse tools/data.Separate trusted instructions from untrusted data; least privilege; tool/action safeguards; validate outputs.
Automation bias / over-reliancePeople accept an AI suggestion without sufficient review.Human accountability, review gates for high-impact decisions, training, and auditability.

Responsible AI: six principles

PrincipleRecognition cue
FairnessBias, equal treatment, discriminatory outcome.
Reliability and safetyRobustness, safeguards, failure handling, monitoring.
Privacy and securityPersonal/sensitive data, access, encryption, retention, protection.
InclusivenessAccessibility, diverse users, different contexts and needs.
TransparencyExplain the basis, limitations, source, or uncertainty of an AI result.
AccountabilityNamed human or organization remains responsible for the outcome.

3. Prompting, grounding, RAG, and model choice

Prompt anatomy

Prompt componentPurposeExample
DirectiveState the task, audience, role, constraints, and desired output.“Summarize this for an executive in five bullets; identify decisions and risks.”
Supporting contentSupply relevant facts, source text, examples, or data.Meeting notes, policy excerpt, table, or approved template.
Output specificationDefine format, length, fields, tone, and quality checks.“Use a table with owner, date, action, confidence, and source.”
One-shot learningProvide one example of the desired transformation or response.Input → ideal output example for a recurring classification task.
Reasoning guidanceAsk the model to use a structured method or verify work.“Compare options against cost, latency, safety, and accuracy; present concise rationale.”

Prompt rule: improve the prompt before changing the model. Be specific, add only relevant context, define a verifiable output, and do not treat an AI response as authoritative without validation.

Choose the right approach

ApproachWhat changesUse it when
Pretrained modelNothing in model weights.A general model meets the need with good prompting and controls.
Prompt engineeringInstructions, context, examples, and output format.You need better task steering, tone, process, or structure.
RAGRuntime context retrieved from governed sources.Knowledge is private, current, frequently changing, or must be traceable to sources.
Fine-tuningModel weights are adapted with a quality task-specific dataset.After prompt/RAG evaluation fails to meet a stable, repeated specialized behavior or format requirement.

Decision rule: Start with pretrained + prompt. Add RAG for knowledge. Fine-tune only for durable behavior, not for weekly policy updates or document lookup.

Model selection signals

NeedModel/capability pattern
Drafting, summarization, Q&A, code, language understandingGeneral-purpose language model.
Complex multi-step analysis, planning, difficult mathematics or codeReasoning model; validate higher quality against extra latency and cost.
Semantic search, similarity, clustering, RAG retrievalEmbedding model + vector/hybrid retrieval; embeddings do not generate the final answer.
Text plus image/audio/video understanding or creationMultimodal model.
High-volume narrow task with cost/latency constraintSmall language model or task-specific model; use the smallest model that meets measured quality.

4. Data, machine learning, security, and cost

Data and grounding checklist

  • Is the data accurate, current, representative, classified, and approved for the intended purpose?
  • Does the user already have permission to access the content? Preserve least privilege and permission trimming.
  • Does a high-impact scenario require human review, source citations, an appeal/escalation route, or audit records?
  • Can the solution minimize sensitive data and apply appropriate retention, redaction, encryption, and logging?

When machine learning adds value

Use ML when historical patterns can improve a forecast, classification, recommendation, anomaly detection, or optimization beyond fixed rules alone.

  1. Frame: define user, decision, KPI, risk, constraints, baseline, and accountable owner.
  2. Prepare data: collect, clean, label, secure, and assess quality/representativeness.
  3. Train and validate: compare candidate models against quality, fairness, robustness, latency, and cost.
  4. Deploy safely: integrate with access controls, monitoring, rollback, user guidance, and human oversight where needed.
  5. Monitor and improve: measure real outcomes, drift, failures, bias, feedback, and retraining needs.

Cost and commercial pattern

QuestionDecision pattern
What drives cost?Tokens/model choice, requests, agent actions, search/indexing, storage, integrations, monitoring, human operations, and training.
Usage uncertain or variable?Pay-as-you-go fits experimentation and variable demand; enforce budgets, tags, quotas, and monitoring.
Usage stable and predictable?Consider commitment/prepaid capacity where commercially appropriate; compare effective cost, not price alone.
Cost rises unexpectedly?Instrument usage, identify the driver, right-size model/retrieval, cache where suitable, and set guardrails before scaling.

Terminology trap: “Copilot Integrator” and “Copilot Graph” are not the usual extension choices. Remember the exam mapping: Graph = work-data platform/context; connector = knowledge; agent = behavior/workflow; action/plugin = API operation.

6. Implement responsibly: strategy, governance, and security

  1. Set strategy and prioritize: select use cases by business value, feasibility, risk, data readiness, and alignment.
  2. Assess readiness and risk: data sensitivity, users impacted, regulatory obligations, harms, model limitations, and human oversight.
  3. Establish minimum controls: approved tools, identity/access, data boundaries, evaluation, incident management, logging, monitoring, and cost controls.
  4. Run a scoped pilot: representative users, one priority workflow, baseline and success metrics, training, feedback, and ownership.
  5. Measure, improve, scale: expand only after demonstrated value, acceptable risk, sustainable operations, and continuous monitoring.

AI council and operating ownership

An AI council is a cross-functional forum for strategic prioritization and oversight; it does not replace delivery teams and it is not only a licensing committee.

AreaTypical contributors
Business value and portfolioExecutive sponsor, business leaders, product/workflow owners, IT leadership.
Responsible AI, legal, compliance, privacyRisk, legal, compliance, privacy, security, data owners.
Technical assuranceEngineering, AI/data teams, enterprise architecture, platform operations.
Adoption and changeChange/adoption team, managers, champions, learning/support functions.

Security and privacy decision cues

  • Identity and permissions: use least privilege; do not let agents bypass the user’s existing authorization.
  • Data protection: classify data, apply sensitivity labels/DLP, minimize sensitive data, define retention, and use approved sources/tools.
  • Application and agent security: validate inputs/outputs, constrain tools/actions, protect secrets, log significant actions, and plan incident response.
  • High-impact decisions: add human oversight, clear accountability, explanation/traceability, validation, and an escalation route.

7. Adoption and measurement

RolePrimary contributionFailure to avoid
Executive sponsorSets direction, funds priorities, removes blockers, reinforces accountability.Leaving AI as an isolated IT experiment.
Adoption/change teamCommunications, role-based learning, support, feedback, benefit measurement, change plan.Measuring success only by training attendance or licenses issued.
AI championsDemonstrate useful scenarios, support peers, surface needs/risks, provide feedback bridge.Treating champions as ungoverned shadow-IT builders or final approvers for high-risk use cases.
Managers/workflow ownersRedesign work, set human review, create time/incentives, own local outcomes.Expecting adoption without workflow/process change.

Meaningful adoption metric: active use in priority workflows plus a measurable business outcome (for example: cycle time, resolution quality, rework, customer satisfaction, compliance, or cost-to-serve). License assignment, launch emails, and training attendance are leading indicators, not proof of value.

Response to low adoption: do not only provide more generic training. Pair a clear use case with role-based practice, in-workflow support, champions, manager sponsorship, approved guardrails, feedback loops, and visible outcome measurement.
