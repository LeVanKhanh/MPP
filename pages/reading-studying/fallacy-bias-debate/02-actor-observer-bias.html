Actor-Observer Bias

We explain our own actions by circumstance — and other people's by character

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Actor-observer bias is the habit of explaining our own behavior by pointing to the situation we're in, while explaining someone else's identical behavior by pointing to who they are. Late for a meeting because of traffic? That's the situation's fault. A colleague late for the same meeting? They must be disorganized. Same lateness, two different stories — and the only thing that changed is whether we're the one acting or the one watching.

The gap comes down to what each side can actually see. As the actor, we're inside the situation — we know about the traffic, the sick kid, the deadline that ate our morning. As the observer, all we have is the visible action; the person's face is the most obvious explanation on offer, so we default to it. Psychologists Edward Jones and Richard Nisbett named this pattern in 1971 and argued it isn't malice — it's a difference in what information each side has access to. [1]

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a corporate scandal it helped explain.

The Same Cut-Off, Two Stories

Imagine two accounts of the same five seconds in traffic. You cut off another driver: you had a work call, missed your exit, panicked, and swerved. You know exactly why — it was situational, a one-off, forgivable. A driver cuts you off: you don't get any of that backstory. All you see is someone swerving in front of you, so the simplest available explanation is that they're reckless, or just a bad driver — not that they're also having a bad five seconds.

Neither account is dishonest. Each side is reporting accurately on what it can see: the actor sees the causes; the observer sees only the result.

What 25 Married Couples Revealed

In 1999, psychologist Astrid Schütz interviewed 25 married couples about real disagreements they had actually had, not hypothetical ones. Across the interviews, the same pattern held: each partner described their own actions during the fight as a reasonable response to the situation, while describing their partner's actions — often the identical behavior — as a matter of character. The asymmetry showed up even in couples who weren't distressed, which suggests it isn't a symptom of a troubled marriage; it's closer to the default way people narrate conflict. [2]

Wells Fargo's Fake-Account Scandal

Between roughly 2002 and 2016, Wells Fargo employees opened millions of accounts customers never asked for, chasing aggressive daily cross-selling quotas. When the scandal broke in 2016, the two sides inside the bank told very different stories about the same behavior. Employees explaining their own conduct pointed to the situation: relentless sales targets, the threat of being fired for missing them, and a bonus structure that rewarded volume over honesty. Bank leadership initially framed the same conduct as the fault of a relatively small number of employees who chose to act unethically — a character explanation, not a situational one — even though later investigations showed executives had been aware of the sales pressure for over a decade. [3] The company's own after-the-fact framing followed the same actor-observer pattern that shows up in a two-driver traffic dispute, just at institutional scale.

A Few Studies on Actor-Observer Bias

Here's what one large-scale review actually studied, what it found, and why that matters for how you use this bias day to day.

The study: In 2006, psychologist Bertram Malle gathered every published test of the actor-observer hypothesis he could find — 173 studies in total, spanning 1971 to 2004 — to check whether the original claim held up: that people always explain their own behavior by circumstance and everyone else's by character, in every kind of situation. [4]

The result: Averaged across every situation in the dataset — trivial daily moments included — the measured asymmetry was close to zero (effect size d = 0.016 to 0.095, where 0 means no bias at all). But when Malle isolated just the studies about negative or undesirable behavior — mistakes, failures, conflicts, situations where someone has to be held responsible — the asymmetry showed up clearly and consistently.

The takeaway: Actor-observer bias isn't a constant setting running in the background of every judgment. It switches on specifically at the moment something has gone wrong and someone needs to be blamed — which is exactly the pattern in the driving, marriage, and Wells Fargo examples above. That makes it more accurate to call this a blame bias than a general attribution bias.

Why it's useful: You don't need to stay on guard against this bias in every conversation — only at specific moments: right after a mistake, an argument, a missed deadline, or a performance review. That's exactly when your brain is most likely to explain someone else's behavior by their character while excusing your own by circumstance. Knowing when that window opens lets you pause before a rushed, unfair judgment — in a disagreement with a partner or in a post-mortem at work.

Mitigation Strategies

Because the bias comes from an information gap — the actor knows the context, the observer only sees the act — the fixes that work are the ones that close that specific gap, not generic empathy exercises.

  • Ask before you attribute: Before deciding someone's action reflects who they are, find out what you don't know about their situation — ask them directly if you can, instead of filling the gap with a guess about their character
  • Apply the rule you'd apply to yourself: If you'd explain your own version of that behavior situationally, extend the same benefit of the doubt to someone else doing it; the test is "would I judge myself this harshly for the same act," not "was this excusable"
  • Investigate the system before the person: Ask "what in the situation made this failure likely?" before asking "who did this?" — this is the structural fix Wells Fargo's leadership skipped when it framed a decade of sales pressure as a personnel problem
  • Save character judgments for patterns, not single incidents: One instance is exactly where actor-observer bias is strongest, because it's when you have the least information about the actor's situation; wait for a pattern across different contexts before concluding anything about who someone is

Summary

Key Takeaway: Actor-observer bias isn't a universal law of judgment — research shows it's weak in neutral situations and sharp specifically around blame, when we're explaining mistakes, conflicts, and failures. The fix isn't vague empathy; it's closing the actual information gap between actor and observer by asking about context before attributing character, and investigating systems before individuals.
