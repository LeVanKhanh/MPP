Anchoring Bias

The first number you hear quietly shapes every judgment that follows

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Anchoring bias is the tendency to lean on the first number we encounter — the "anchor" — when judging something, even when that number has nothing to do with the real answer. Once an anchor is in our head, every estimate we make afterward tends to drift toward it instead of starting fresh. A jacket tagged "was $60, now $25" feels like a steal; the exact same $25 jacket with no crossed-out price just feels like a jacket.

The mechanism is called anchoring-and-adjustment: we take the first number as a starting point and adjust away from it, but the adjustment is almost always too small. Psychologists Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman named the effect in 1974, and their experiments showed something unsettling — the anchor doesn't even have to be relevant or credible to work. [1]

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a corporate blunder it helps explain.

The Same Candidate, Two Starting Salaries

Imagine two equally qualified candidates negotiating for the identical job. Candidate A's recruiter opens with "the budget is around $60,000." Candidate B's recruiter opens with "we're thinking somewhere near $90,000." Both candidates push back reasonably and negotiate in good faith — but Candidate A settles near $65,000 and Candidate B settles near $82,000. A $17,000 gap for identical qualifications, explained entirely by which number entered the room first.

21 Real Estate Agents, One House

In 1987, researchers Gregory Northcraft and Margaret Neale gave real estate agents and college students a full walk-through of the same house, along with a genuine ten-page packet of comparable sales data — the same information any professional would use to set a price. The only thing that changed between groups was the listing price printed at the top of the packet. Even though every agent toured the same rooms and read the same comparables, their final appraisals tracked the manipulated listing price rather than the underlying data alone. Experience didn't cancel out the anchor — the professional real estate agents were just as anchored as the undergraduates were. [2]

How J.C. Penney Lost Nearly $1 Billion by Removing Its Anchors

In 2012, J.C. Penney's new CEO Ron Johnson scrapped the retailer's entire "was $60, now $25" pricing system, replacing thousands of yearly sales and coupons with simple, permanently low, "fair and square" prices — no crossed-out numbers, no reference point to measure a discount against. Many of the new prices were mathematically similar to the old sale prices. But customers no longer felt like they were getting a deal, because the anchor they used to judge a deal against was gone. Comparable store sales fell 18.9% in the first quarter, then 21.7% in the second, then 31.7% by the fourth quarter; the company posted a net loss of $985 million for the year and reversed the strategy in 2013. [3] The failure is now taught as a business case precisely because it demonstrates anchoring bias from the other direction: take the anchor away, and perceived value collapses with it.

A Few Studies on Anchoring Bias

Here's the experiment that gave this bias its name, what it found, and why the result still matters today.

The study: In 1974, Tversky and Kahneman ran an experiment with nothing to do with negotiating or shopping, designed specifically to test whether an anchor could bias judgment even when it was obviously meaningless. In front of each participant, they spun a wheel of fortune rigged to stop on either 10 or 65. Then they asked a completely unrelated question: what percentage of countries in the United Nations are African? [1]

The result: Participants who saw the wheel land on 10 guessed an average of 25%. Participants who saw it land on 65 guessed an average of 45% — nearly double, based on a number everyone had just watched a rigged wheel spit out at random.

The takeaway: An anchor doesn't need to be relevant, credible, or even remotely connected to the question to move your estimate. Anchoring isn't really a reasoning error about trusting bad information — it's closer to a reflex that fires whenever a number enters your head first, before you've had any chance to judge whether it deserves weight at all.

Why it's useful: This changes what to watch for. You don't only need to be wary of anchors that sound credible — a random price tag, an offhand comment, or an opening offer with zero basis can still shift your judgment. The practical habit is noticing the first number in any decision and asking, before you commit to an estimate, "would I land somewhere different if I'd heard a different number first?"

Mitigation Strategies

Anchoring works by hijacking your starting point, so the fixes that work are the ones that control which number you start from.

  • Move first when you have better information: In a negotiation, whoever states a number first sets the anchor the other side adjusts from — if you know the market, open first instead of reacting to their number
  • Form your own estimate before seeing anyone else's: Write down your independent number — a salary figure, a price, a probability — before you look at a listing price, an opening offer, or someone else's guess, so that number can't quietly become your anchor
  • Deliberately argue the opposite: Before accepting a number, spend a minute listing concrete reasons it could be too high, then reasons it could be too low — this "consider-the-opposite" step is one of the few techniques shown in lab studies to actually shrink the anchoring effect
  • Ask where the number came from: If you can't explain why a specific figure is relevant to your decision — a rigged wheel spin, an arbitrary opening offer, a price you happened to see yesterday — treat it as noise, not information

Summary

Key Takeaway: Anchoring bias shows that the first number we hear shapes our judgment even when that number is random, irrelevant, or set by someone else's interest rather than ours. The real defense isn't "try to ignore the number" — it's controlling which number goes first: forming your own independent estimate, opening negotiations yourself when you can, and deliberately checking whether a number deserves any weight before you let it anchor you.
