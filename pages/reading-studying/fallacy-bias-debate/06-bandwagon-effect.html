Bandwagon Effect

The more people already believe something, the easier it becomes to believe it too

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

The bandwagon effect is the tendency to adopt a belief, product, or behavior simply because a growing number of other people already have — treating popularity itself as evidence, separate from whatever facts actually support the belief. The more people appear to already be on board, the more "obviously right" joining them feels, even when nothing about the underlying reality has changed.

This runs on a shortcut called social proof: when we're uncertain, we use other people's behavior as a stand-in for our own judgment, on the assumption that a crowd that large can't all be wrong. It's often a reasonable strategy — but it also means popularity can become self-reinforcing, growing purely because it has already grown, independent of merit.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a market frenzy it helps explain.

Two Restaurants Side by Side, Only One With a Line

Imagine two restaurants next door to each other, nearly identical menus, comparable food quality. The one on the left sits empty. The one on the right, for some random reason — maybe just a group that arrived early and had to wait — starts to form a short line outside. Passersby see the line and think, "that one must be better, or why else would people be waiting?" They join the line. The line grows longer, which draws even more people to join it. By the end of the night, the restaurant on the right is full and the one on the left is still empty — not because the food was better, but because the original line had become its own evidence.

The Paper That Named the Effect

In 1954, economist and political scientist Herbert Simon — who would later win the Nobel Prize in Economics — published the paper that gave this effect its name. He was trying to answer a practical question: if pre-election polls report Candidate A is ahead, does publishing that number itself push more voters toward Candidate A, purely because they now look likely to win? Simon called this the "bandwagon effect" — voters shifting toward whichever candidate they expect to win, separate from whether they actually support that candidate's positions. [1]

When 900,000 Accounts Piled Into the Same Stock

In January 2021, GameStop's stock price rose from around $17 to over $500 in a matter of weeks — nearly 30 times higher. The wave started on the Reddit forum r/WallStreetBets, where a growing number of individual investors bought in simply because they saw a growing number of other people buying in and posting their gains. The number of accounts trading GameStop rose from about 10,000 at the start of the month to roughly 900,000 at the peak — a 90-fold increase in a few weeks. [2] In its official report released later that year, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission concluded that "positive sentiment" — not hedge funds being forced to buy back shares they had shorted — was what actually sustained the weeks-long price climb. In other words: the price rose because more and more people believed it would keep rising, and that belief fed itself.

A Few Studies on the Bandwagon Effect

Here's the classic experiment behind why social proof works even against your own eyes, what it found, and what it's actually useful for knowing.

The study: In 1951, psychologist Solomon Asch wanted to test whether a person would say something obviously false simply because everyone around them said it first. He gave participants an extremely simple task: compare the length of lines and point out which one matched a reference line — a task with an answer almost impossible to get wrong. But in each group, only one person was a real participant; everyone else was an actor instructed to unanimously give an obviously wrong answer on certain rounds. [3]

The result: With no group pressure, participants almost never answered incorrectly — under 1% of the time. But when the group around them unanimously picked the wrong answer, 75% of participants went along with the group at least once, and across all the pressured rounds, they conformed to the group's wrong answer about 37% of the time — despite seeing the correct answer with their own eyes.

The takeaway: Group pressure is strong enough to make people state something that contradicts what they can plainly see, with no threats or coercion involved — just a reluctance to be the one person in the room who's different. That's the core of the bandwagon effect: social proof can outweigh the evidence right in front of you.

Why it's useful: This gives you a concrete number to remember: even on a question this simple, three out of four people abandoned their own judgment at least once to go along with the group. If that happens on something as easy as comparing line lengths, it's far more likely on the ambiguous calls people actually face — which product to buy, who to trust, which candidate to support. Knowing that in advance is useful, because it means the feeling of "this must be right, since everyone thinks so" isn't always as trustworthy as it feels.

Mitigation Strategies

The bandwagon effect works by substituting other people's behavior for your own judgment, so the fixes that work are the ones that protect that judgment before the crowd gets a vote.

  • Separate "popular" from "correct": Before deciding, ask yourself privately — if I had no idea anyone else was choosing this, would I still choose it?
  • Form your opinion before you see the crowd's: Write down or commit to your independent judgment before a group discussion, a comment section, or a poll result can anchor you to what everyone else already thinks
  • Find at least one dissenting voice: In Asch's experiments, having just one other person also give the correct answer was enough to help participants hold onto their own judgment — actively seek out one contrary opinion before you decide
  • Distinguish "popular because proven" from "popular because popular": Ask whether an impressive number — views, a line out the door, a rising stock price — comes with any actual evidence behind it, or whether the popularity is simply feeding itself

Summary

Key Takeaway: The bandwagon effect drives us to treat popularity itself as evidence, adopting beliefs and behaviors because a crowd already has — even when that crowd grew for reasons that have nothing to do with merit. The real defense isn't willpower against following trends; it's forming an independent judgment before the crowd's choice becomes visible, and asking whether a number that looks impressive is actually backed by anything besides its own momentum.
