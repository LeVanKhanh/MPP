Confirmation Bias

We don't search for the truth — we search for evidence we were right all along

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, notice, interpret, and remember information in a way that supports what we already believe, while overlooking or explaining away anything that doesn't. It rarely feels like bias from the inside — it feels like being right, because the evidence you're finding really does agree with you. What's easy to miss is how much you steered the search that found it.

This shows up at three separate points, not just one: in what we search for (we phrase questions in ways more likely to return results we already agree with), in how we interpret ambiguous evidence (the same data reads as support if it matches our view and as noise if it doesn't), and in what we remember later (agreeing information simply sticks better than disagreeing information). Each stage reinforces the other two, which is part of why the bias is so hard to catch from inside your own head.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a disaster it helped explain.

The Coworker Labeled "Difficult" in Week One

Imagine a new hire joins a team, and in their first week, they push back hard on an idea in a meeting. The team quietly files them under "difficult." From then on, every time this person asks a question or raises a concern, coworkers read it as "there they go again" — while the times they compliment an idea, quietly agree, or defer to someone else go unnoticed and unremembered. After a few months, the team is "sure" this person is difficult — not because they act differently from anyone else, but because every one of their actions got filtered through the same interpretation locked in during week one.

The Puzzle That Named the Bias

In 1960, psychologist Peter Wason showed participants the number sequence 2-4-6, told them it followed a rule, and asked them to figure out the rule by proposing their own triples and getting a "fits" or "doesn't fit" response each time. Most people instantly formed the hypothesis "even numbers, ascending by two" and then only tested triples that matched it — like 8-10-12 or 20-22-24 — getting confirmed every time. Very few people tried a triple specifically designed to break their hypothesis, like 1-2-3 or 6-5-4; if they had, they'd have discovered the actual rule was much simpler: any three ascending numbers. Of the 29 participants in the original study, only 6 landed on the correct rule without making a wrong guess first. [1] Participants weren't short on logic — they simply never thought to look for evidence that could prove them wrong, and that exact blind spot is what later gave this bias its name.

The Night Before Challenger

The night before the Space Shuttle Challenger launched in January 1986, engineers at contractor Morton Thiokol warned that unusually cold temperatures in Florida could cause the rubber O-rings sealing its rocket boosters to lose flexibility and leak hot gas. They recommended against launching below 53°F, the coldest temperature at which a shuttle had previously launched safely. According to later testimony before the Rogers Commission, rather than being asked to prove the launch was safe, the engineers found themselves pressed to prove the opposite — that it was unsafe — reversing the normal burden of proof onto the people trying to stop a decision that already seemed all but made. Morton Thiokol's management ultimately reversed its own engineers' recommendation and approved the launch. Challenger exploded 73 seconds after liftoff, killing all seven astronauts aboard. [2] The Rogers Commission found that both NASA and Morton Thiokol had known about the O-ring risk for years, repeatedly interpreting ambiguous data in whichever direction supported the conclusion they already wanted: that it was safe to fly.

A Few Studies on Confirmation Bias

Here's a study that tested a common intuition — that showing two opposing sides the same evidence should bring them closer together — and found the opposite.

The study: In 1979, researchers Charles Lord, Lee Ross, and Mark Lepper at Stanford wanted to test a specific question: if you show two opposing groups the exact same evidence, does it pull their views closer together? They recruited strong supporters and strong opponents of capital punishment and had everyone read the same two fabricated studies — one supporting the idea that capital punishment deters crime, one refuting it. [3]

The result: Both sides rated the study that agreed with their existing view as far more convincing than the one that didn't — even though the two studies were built with comparable methodological quality. More strikingly, when asked to compare their current attitude to their attitude before the experiment, supporters reported feeling even more strongly in favor of capital punishment, and opponents reported feeling even more strongly against it — after reading the exact same packet of material.

The takeaway: Handing mixed evidence to two opposing groups didn't bring them closer together — it pushed them further apart. Neither side read the evidence neutrally; each read it through an existing filter, accepting the part that agreed with them and finding fault with the rest. This pattern was named "biased assimilation," and it explains why arguing with data alone so rarely changes anyone's mind.

Why it's useful: This is useful because it corrects a common, wrong intuition — that piling on more data, statistics, or evidence is enough to convince someone who disagrees. On topics where people already hold strong positions, mixed evidence often backfires. If the actual goal is to change a mind — someone else's, or your own — knowing this in advance means designing a different approach than simply adding more numbers to a stalemate.

Mitigation Strategies

Confirmation bias works at the search, interpretation, and memory stages, so the fixes that work are the ones aimed at those exact points, not general open-mindedness.

  • Actively try to disprove yourself, not just confirm: Before concluding something, ask "what would I need to see to know I'm wrong?" and then deliberately go look for that specific thing, instead of gathering more examples that agree with you
  • Reverse the burden of proof before high-stakes calls: Before an irreversible decision, ask "what evidence would I need to see to catch it if the opposite were true?" — instead of implicitly requiring the dissenting view to prove itself beyond doubt, which is exactly the trap Challenger's engineers were pushed into
  • Write down your prediction before you know the outcome: Commit to what you expect will happen before you see the result, then check it against what actually happened — this keeps memory from only holding on to the times you were right
  • Don't just add more data to a polarized argument: Lord, Ross, and Lepper's research shows mixed evidence can push opposing sides further apart, not closer together — if the goal is actually to persuade, look for shared values first instead of stacking more statistics onto an already rigid disagreement

Summary

Key Takeaway: Confirmation bias operates at three points — search, interpretation, and memory — which is why it feels invisible from the inside: the evidence you notice genuinely does agree with you. The real defense isn't vague open-mindedness; it's deliberately searching for what would prove you wrong, reversing the burden of proof before high-stakes decisions, and recognizing that piling more evidence onto an already polarized disagreement can push people further apart instead of closer together.
