Groupthink

The tighter a group agrees, the less likely anyone checks whether it's actually right

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Groupthink is what happens when a group's desire to preserve harmony and consensus overrides its members' willingness to realistically evaluate alternatives. It isn't the same as simple peer pressure — it's a group actively suppressing its own doubts, individually and collectively, because raising them feels like it would damage a unity everyone has come to depend on. Psychologist Irving Janis coined the term in 1972 after studying how President Kennedy's advisors approved the disastrous 1961 Bay of Pigs invasion, even though several of them later admitted to having privately doubted the plan at the time. [1]

Janis identified several recurring symptoms that reinforce each other inside a groupthink-prone team, summarized below.

SymptomWhat it looks like
Illusion of invulnerability"We've never been wrong before, this will probably work out too"
Self-censorshipHaving a doubt but not voicing it, assuming "I must be the only one who sees this"
Illusion of unanimitySilence in the room gets read as "everyone agrees"
MindguardingA member quietly filters dissenting information before it reaches the leader, "to avoid bothering them"

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a corporate disaster it helps explain.

The Hiring Meeting Where No One Says What They Actually Think

Imagine a five-person hiring committee meeting to choose between two finalists. The most senior person in the room speaks first: "I really like Candidate A." Two of the remaining four actually lean toward Candidate B, but when it's their turn to speak, both say "I think A is pretty solid too" — not because they changed their minds, but because neither wants to be the only one pushing back on the boss in the room. The committee "unanimously" picks Candidate A. No one lied, no one was threatened — but the final decision reflects one person's opinion, co-signed by five names.

The Experiment That Tested What Actually Drives Groupthink

In 1977, researcher Margaret Flowers ran the first lab experiment to directly test Janis's theory. She split college students into small groups, gave each group a simulated crisis to solve, and varied two factors independently: how cohesive the group was (high or low) and the leadership style (open, encouraging debate, versus directive, steering the group toward a preset conclusion). Groups with open leadership generated more solutions and used more available information during discussion, regardless of how cohesive the group was — while Janis's original claim that tighter group bonds alone drive worse decisions found no clear support in the data. [2] Leadership style, not closeness between members, turned out to be the stronger predictor.

The Word Boeing Avoided Saying Out Loud

During development of the Boeing 737 MAX, some engineers and test pilots raised concerns about a new automated flight-control system called MCAS — including one engineer who watched a pilot struggle to control the plane in a simulator and questioned why MCAS kept activating, and another who questioned the system's reliance on a single angle-of-attack sensor. According to the later investigation by the U.S. House Committee on Transportation and Infrastructure, these warnings were "not thoroughly investigated or dismissed." Investigators also uncovered a 2013 internal record of a meeting where employees discussed avoiding the term "MCAS" with anyone outside the company, reasoning that emphasizing the new system might "increase certification and training impact." [3] Two 737 MAX jets crashed within five months of each other — Lion Air Flight 610 in October 2018 and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302 in March 2019 — killing 346 people combined. It wasn't that no one inside the company knew about the problem; it's that the internal culture had made staying quiet feel safer than speaking up.

A Few Studies on Groupthink

Here's a review that asked whether Janis's full theory actually matches real-world decisions, what it found, and how that changes what to watch for.

The study: In 1998, researcher James Esser reviewed the full 25 years of experimental studies and historical case analyses of groupthink published since Janis's theory, to answer a specific question: does Janis's complete model — every symptom appearing together, all driving straight toward disaster — actually match what happens in real groups? [4]

The result: Esser found genuinely mixed evidence. Some individual symptoms — particularly self-censorship and the presence of a "mindguard" — did correlate with worse decisions across many studies. But Janis's full model, where every symptom appears together in one "perfect storm" leading directly to catastrophe, was rarely reproduced exactly, either in lab experiments or under close scrutiny of the historical cases Janis originally used.

The takeaway: Groupthink doesn't operate like an on/off switch — a group either fully in its grip or entirely unaffected. In practice, individual symptoms can show up on their own, and each one alone is enough to degrade decision quality, without the complete "symptom checklist" Janis originally described.

Why it's useful: This matters because it means you don't need to wait until you spot the full classic pattern before getting concerned. Noticing just one warning sign — say, someone quietly filtering dissenting information before it reaches the decision-maker, as happened with MCAS at Boeing — is already reason enough to intervene, without waiting for the entire textbook disaster scenario to become visible.

Mitigation Strategies

Since leadership style, not closeness between members, turned out to be the strongest lever in the research, the fixes that work are aimed there first.

  • Leaders speak last, not first: Flowers's research found leadership style shapes discussion quality more than group cohesion does — if you're the most senior person in the room, let everyone else weigh in before stating your own view
  • Assign a formal dissenter, not an accidental one: Give the "devil's advocate" role to a specific person for each important meeting, so disagreeing stops being a risky individual act and becomes an expected job
  • Collect opinions privately before discussing them openly: Have each person write down their view before hearing anyone else's — this directly breaks the "illusion of unanimity," since silence in a meeting room never actually meant agreement
  • Treat one warning sign as sufficient to pause: Per Esser's review, you don't need the full symptom checklist before acting — if you notice someone filtering out dissenting information before it reaches the decision-maker, as happened with MCAS, that alone is a strong enough signal to stop and reassess

Summary

Key Takeaway: Groupthink happens when a group's need for harmony quietly overrides its members' willingness to voice doubts, and research shows it doesn't need every classic symptom present at once — even one, like a single person filtering out dissent, is enough to degrade a decision. The real defense is structural: leaders speaking last, a formally assigned dissenter, and collecting opinions privately before the group ever gets a chance to look unanimous.
