Optimism Bias

We take in good news eagerly and shrug off bad news about ourselves

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Optimism bias is the tendency to believe we're less likely than other people to experience negative events — an accident, a divorce, a serious illness — and more likely to experience positive ones, even when nothing about our actual circumstances justifies the gap. It isn't simple hopefulness; it's a specific, measurable asymmetry: ask people to rate their own risk against the average person's, and most rate themselves as safer than the math allows for everyone to be at once.

One reason this persists: when people get good news about their future risk, they update their beliefs quickly to match it. When they get bad news, they barely adjust at all. The same brain that eagerly absorbs "you're less likely to get this than you thought" tends to shrug off "you're more likely than you thought" — an imbalance in how new information gets processed, not just a matter of temperament.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a corporate collapse it helps explain.

The Newlyweds and the Statistic They Don't Think Applies to Them

Imagine a newly married couple watching a news segment reporting that roughly 40-50% of marriages in the U.S. end in divorce. Both nod along — that number sounds about right, for other people. Neither actually believes it applies to their own marriage. Mathematically, this can't be true for everyone at once: if everyone thinks they're the exception, millions of people are simultaneously right about themselves and wrong about each other.

The Study That Named the Bias

In 1980, psychologist Neil Weinstein surveyed 258 college students, asking them to compare their own chances of experiencing 42 different life events — 18 positive ones (owning a home, living past 80) and 24 negative ones (getting divorced, being fired, having a heart attack) — against their classmates. The result: students consistently rated their own odds as above average for positive events and below average for negative ones. [1] In a follow-up study, when Weinstein had students list the reasons they thought their own chances were better, then had a second group read those exact reasons, unrealistic optimism dropped significantly — but didn't disappear. The problem wasn't a lack of logic; it was that each person could clearly see the factors working in their own favor, while forgetting that everyone else has comparable factors working in theirs.

The $1.75 Billion Bet That Lasted Six Months

In 2020, the short-form video platform Quibi launched with $1.75 billion in funding from major backers including Disney, NBCUniversal, and WarnerMedia, led by Jeffrey Katzenberg (former CEO of DreamWorks) and Meg Whitman (former CEO of eBay and HP). The company projected more than 7 million paying subscribers within its first year. According to people close to the company, leadership brushed aside warnings from advisors about the business model and market approach. A few weeks before shutting down, Quibi had roughly 500,000 paying subscribers — less than a fourteenth of its confident forecast. The company announced it was shutting down about six months after launch, despite raising one of the largest startup war chests in history. Enormous funding and experienced leadership offered no immunity to optimism bias — if anything, a strong track record made it easier to believe the usual risks simply wouldn't apply this time.

A Few Studies on Optimism Bias

Here's a study that tried to pin down exactly why this bias survives repeated exposure to contradicting information.

The study: In 2011, neuroscientist Tali Sharot and colleagues wanted to identify precisely what process keeps optimism bias so persistent, even when people are repeatedly exposed to contradicting information. They asked participants to estimate their own probability of experiencing various negative life events — a specific illness, a burglary, a divorce — then told them the actual average probability for someone of their age and background, which was sometimes higher and sometimes lower than their original guess. Finally, they measured how much participants revised their own estimate after learning the real number. [2]

The result: When given "good news" — the real risk was lower than they'd guessed — participants adjusted their beliefs sharply, nearly adopting the new number outright. When given "bad news" — the real risk was higher than they'd guessed — they barely adjusted at all, despite receiving the exact same kind of reliable information either way.

The takeaway: This is the mechanism that keeps optimism bias so durable: it's not that people never hear the warning — it's that the brain eagerly absorbs good news about itself and largely shrugs off bad news, even when both come from an equally credible source.

Why it's useful: This is useful because it explains why simply adding more warnings, statistics, or advice often isn't enough to change someone's risky behavior — including your own. The problem isn't a lack of information; it's that the brain processes good and bad news about itself through two fundamentally different channels. Knowing this in advance explains why "just pointing out the risk" rarely works on its own, and why external mechanisms — hard deadlines with real consequences, public commitments, contractual obligations — are often needed to offset this natural tendency.

Mitigation Strategies

Optimism bias works by absorbing good news and discounting bad news about your own risk, so the fixes that work are the ones that don't depend on how the risk "feels."

  • Don't rely on "knowing the risk" alone — build in external constraints: Because the brain naturally discounts bad news about its own risk, don't count on willpower after seeing the statistics — put concrete constraints in place (deadlines with real penalties, public commitments to other people) that don't depend on whether you "feel" at risk
  • List the factors working in other people's favor too: Weinstein's research found that having people list the advantages other people also have — not just their own — significantly reduced unrealistic optimism; before a major decision, try listing why someone else in a similar situation could reasonably fail too
  • Find an advisor with actual veto power, and genuinely listen: Quibi shows that money and experience offer no immunity to optimism bias — if leadership (or you) can simply overrule every warning, give someone real veto authority, not just a seat to offer opinions that can be ignored
  • Treat "bad news" and "good news" about your own risk the same way: When you get both good and bad news about the same risk — a health test, market feedback — ask whether you're adjusting your belief far less in response to the bad news than the good; if so, that's optimism bias operating in real time

Summary

Key Takeaway: Optimism bias means people absorb good news about their own risk far more readily than bad news, which is why warnings alone rarely change behavior — including in well-funded, experienced teams. The real defense isn't more statistics; it's building external constraints that don't depend on how safe something feels, actively listing the advantages other people share too, and giving someone genuine authority to say no before overconfidence turns into an unrecoverable bet.
