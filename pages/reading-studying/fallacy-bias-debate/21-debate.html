Debate: Both Sides Win

Not every argument has a winner and a loser

Updated: July 21, 2026

You Can't Win An Argument

There's a line from Dale Carnegie I keep coming back to: "You can't win an argument." Arguments happen everywhere in life, and everyone who steps into one quietly believes they're right, that they'll be the one left standing at the end. But looked at through the lens of cognitive bias, that belief turns out to be far less solid than it feels.

Winning the Argument, Losing the Relationship

Even when you win an argument, you can still lose the relationship it was fought in. Carnegie's proposed solution was: "The only way to get the best of an argument is to avoid it." That holds true when the goal is to keep the peace, to compromise. But there's another side to arguing — one that helps us get a little better every day. So, as I see it, the answer isn't to avoid debate altogether, but to follow a different rule instead: "Both sides win."

Zero-Sum Games and Positive-Sum Games

The difference between a contest worth avoiding and one worth seeking out comes down to this: does the total value of the outcome equal zero, or is it greater than zero?

When the Total Is Zero

In a contest that has to end with a winner and a loser — where the total value of the outcome is zero — the only meaning lies in getting to compete at all: the classic Tortoise and the Hare race, a game of chess, a football match, a head-to-head fight. If one side wins, the other has to lose, and one side's gain is exactly the other side's loss.

When the Total Is Greater Than Zero

But in everyday life and work, we should be looking for "contests" whose value is always greater than zero, so that everyone wins, so the value never disappears and can even grow through the contest itself. That's why modern retellings of the Tortoise and the Hare exist now — versions where both cross the finish line together. Along the same stretch of road, we can absolutely get what we want, together.

Ego: The Hidden Barrier Behind Zero-Sum Thinking

There's a question worth pausing on before going further: why does a debate that could be positive-sum so easily slide into zero-sum? Often, the barrier isn't the issue being discussed at all — it's a quieter, more defensive reflex: the feeling of ego under threat.

When Disagreement Becomes a Personal Threat

An opinion getting rejected is, at bottom, just an opinion getting rejected. But we rarely process it that simply. It easily slides into "I got rejected," "I'm inadequate," "I'm under attack."

Researcher Dan Kahan calls a mechanism close to this "identity-protective cognition." His original research is about group scale: people tend to process evidence in ways that protect their cultural or political identity, or their attachment to a group they belong to, rather than in ways that lead to the truth. Applying that idea to the individual scale is an extrapolation, not a direct conclusion of the research — but it suggests something worth considering: the same defensive mechanism may also operate at a smaller scale, where what's under threat isn't a group identity, but one's own image of competence.

Once the ego feels threatened that way, the natural reflex is to shift from finding-a-shared-solution to defending-oneself. That's the moment a debate that could have been positive-sum gets dragged back to zero-sum — not because the issue demanded it, but because emotion quietly changed the rules of the game.

Recognizing It in Yourself

A few signals worth watching for in yourself, before the reflex outruns reason:

  • A faster heartbeat, a louder voice, or the urge to interrupt before the other person finishes
  • Already rehearsing a rebuttal in your head, instead of actually listening to what they're saying
  • The feeling that conceding one small point would mean admitting total defeat
  • Wanting to win to prove you're smart or valuable, more than wanting to find what's true

The fix, maybe, isn't trying to suppress that feeling, but separating it from the argument. "This opinion of mine is wrong" is a completely different sentence from "I am wrong, I am inadequate." Just naming that difference, in the moment, is often enough to cool most of the defensive reflex.

A simple question helps too: "am I rebutting this because the argument is weak, or because I feel threatened?" That question creates a small pause. The technique of restating the other side's argument before rebutting it — covered in more detail below — plays a similar role: it forces you to listen before you're allowed to react, and in that delay, the ego usually has time to cool down.

What If the Other Side Has a High Ego? Is Yielding Always the Answer?

The short answer, maybe, is: not quite. It's worth telling two kinds of yielding apart.

The first kind is a deliberate choice, made to step out of a zero-sum standstill — as noted above, this is exactly when Carnegie's "avoid it" advice is right. The second kind is yielding purely because you're uncomfortable with conflict, even when the issue genuinely matters. This kind doesn't resolve anything. It just postpones the problem, and usually leaves a quiet residue of resentment.

Research on "threatened egotism" by Roy Baumeister and colleagues found that when a highly favorable self-view is challenged, the common response is aggression, not revising that self-view. The research doesn't directly say that yielding to a high-ego person now means a bigger reaction next time — that's an extrapolation, not the study's conclusion. But it does suggest something worth weighing: if a person's view has never been genuinely tested, their favorable (and possibly inaccurate) view of themselves has never had a chance to be corrected either.

So the real question is probably not "should I yield or fight this out," but: does yielding this time actually solve the problem, or is it just avoiding the discomfort of this particular moment?

A middle path, proposed by negotiation researchers Roger Fisher and William Ury, is separating the person from their position — giving the other side a way to accept a better solution without paying for it with their sense of self. A few concrete ways: phrasing what you want to say as a question rather than a statement, so they arrive at the conclusion themselves instead of feeling forced into defeat; publicly acknowledging what they got right before raising what needs adjusting; or simply moving the conversation somewhere private, since ego tends to feel more threatened with an audience watching.

Yielding is meaningful when it's a deliberate choice, made to preserve something more important — a relationship, time, energy. But if yielding is just a way of avoiding, it isn't a solution — it's a debt left for later.

Debate Is a Tool, Not a Battlefield

In research, engineering, or decision-making, figuring out which claim is true still matters — that's not something to avoid. What's worth avoiding is something else: conflating the rightness or wrongness of an argument with a win or loss for the person making it. Debate, here, is a tool for finding a better argument together, not an arena for deciding who comes out on top.

Three Familiar Situations

The idea of "both sides win" sounds a little idealistic when it's just said out loud. But look at a few concrete situations, and it stops feeling abstract — it's already quietly happening in places we rarely think to call "debate."

A Comment in Code Review

When a developer leaves a comment disagreeing with how a piece of code was written, what follows is rarely called a "debate" — but that's exactly what it is. Nobody wins if the final code still has a bug in it; the merged pull request belongs to both people, and who was right in which comment thread stops mattering. Maybe that's why good engineering teams rarely remember who "won" a review — they just remember that the code got better.

A Peer Review in Scientific Research

Peer review is a required ritual before a piece of research gets accepted. The reviewer doesn't need to "defeat" the author; and the author, if the review turns up a real flaw, hasn't "lost" either — they've just been saved from publishing something wrong. What gets raised, in the end, is shared understanding, not any one person's reputation.

A Salary Negotiation

Salary negotiations are usually pictured as a tug of war: the employee wants more, the company wants to pay less, and whoever gives ground first loses. But if both sides ask a different question instead — how much value is this person actually creating, and what compensation structure genuinely reflects that value — the final number stops being a reluctant midpoint between two opposing expectations, and becomes the result of both sides seeing something more clearly together. The economic theory of comparative advantage suggests a similar intuition at a much larger scale: two parties trade not because one is forced to concede, but because the exchange itself creates value that didn't exist for either of them before.

Principles For An Effective Debate

  • Prove yourself right, not just the other side wrong: the other side being wrong doesn't automatically make you right — your own argument still needs to stand on its own evidence, independent of whether theirs has collapsed
  • Target the argument, not the person making it: once the back-and-forth starts touching the person instead of the idea, the debate has probably quietly changed its target
  • Keep the argument standing on evidence, not assumption: an unchecked assumption, if never named as one, is easy to treat as if it were already fact

Strengthen the Other Side's Argument Before You Rebut It

There's a small test worth trying before rebutting anything: restate the other person's argument in its strongest possible form — stronger, even, than how they presented it themselves. If they hear it and nod, "yes, that's exactly what I meant," only then are you actually engaging with what they think, rather than knocking down an easier version you built yourself. The philosopher Daniel Dennett calls these four steps "Rapoport's Rules" (named after the game theorist Anatol Rapoport): restate the other side's position so well they say "thanks, I wish I'd put it that way myself"; list the points you agree on; mention what you've learned from them; and only then are you allowed to say what you disagree with.

Separate "Being Right" From "Winning"

Leaving a debate with a clearer understanding — even one that means correcting something you used to believe — is still a kind of win, just one nobody claps for. Maybe the real measure of a good debate isn't who walks out with their head held higher, but whether both people walk out seeing things more clearly than when they walked in.

A Debate About Debate Itself

Even Dale Carnegie's advice to "avoid the argument" hasn't stood still — it's been revisited over time.

Original claim: Carnegie argued that arguments rarely change anyone's mind — people cling to their opinions out of ego more than logic, so avoidance is the most practical way to preserve both the relationship and the peace.

Counterpoint: Research on high-performing teams tells a different story. Researcher Amy Edmondson calls the key condition behind those teams "psychological safety" — a shared belief within a team that taking interpersonal risks, like asking questions, admitting mistakes, or raising disagreements, won't get anyone punished or thought less of. When Google studied internally what made a team effective (a project called Project Aristotle), psychological safety was one of five dynamics identified, and ranked as the most important of the five — not the absence of disagreement, but disagreement being allowed to happen safely.

Where things stand: Maybe both are right, just under different conditions. Carnegie is right when a debate is pictured as a public contest with a winner and a loser, where ego is on the line from the start. The research on high-performing teams is right when the debate happens in a space safe enough for that kind of interpersonal risk-taking. The real difference, it turns out, isn't whether debate happens at all — it's the atmosphere around it.

A Few Things Worth Asking Myself

Not a guaranteed formula — just a few things I find worth asking before stepping into a debate.

  • Agree on the problem to solve, and what "winning" means for both sides: if neither side can define that from the start, the debate will drift toward someone having to lose
  • Ask to understand, before rebutting: a clarifying question often reveals that what you're about to object to isn't quite what the other person was saying
  • Start from a point of agreement, then move to disagreement: this small ordering choice often decides which way the whole conversation goes
  • When emotion crowds out the ability to listen, pause and come back later: a conversation resumed once both sides are calmer is usually better than a line said that can't be taken back

When the Other Side Doesn't Want Both Sides to Win

The section above was about saving face for a high-ego opponent, in one specific moment — that's the approach for when the issue is still worth working out together. But there's a different situation: when a threatened ego isn't a single moment, but a repeating pattern — and that pattern shows the other side isn't actually aiming for a mutual win. The "both sides win" principle assumes the other person is also looking for a positive-sum game, the same way you are. That assumption doesn't always hold, and it's probably worth recognizing that sooner rather than later.

A Few Signs Worth Noticing

There's no single tell, but a few things, if they show up together, are worth stopping to look at more closely:

  • The back-and-forth shifts from arguments to personal attacks
  • The other person is waiting for their turn to speak, not actually listening
  • The goalposts keep moving — every time one point gets resolved, a new one gets set up
  • It feels like the other person needs to win in order to feel valuable, more than they need a better solution

That's When Carnegie's Original Advice Is Right Again

When those signs show up, this is probably exactly the moment Carnegie's original advice returns to where it belongs: the best way to get the most out of a debate like this is to avoid it. Not because walking away is weak, but because a zero-sum game doesn't turn into a positive-sum one just because one side wants it to — both sides have to agree to step into the same game before it can become one.

Avoiding it, here, doesn't have to mean walking away in silence. It might mean moving the conversation to a different format — writing it out instead of going back and forth live, so emotion crowds out the argument less. It might mean setting a boundary: saying clearly what you're still willing to discuss, and what you're done arguing about. And sometimes it's simply accepting that not every disagreement needs to be resolved right then — walking away from a debate that has no shared way to win isn't a failure on your part.

Summary

Key Point: Not every argument is worth avoiding — only zero-sum contests, where one side's win is exactly the other side's loss, call for Carnegie's advice to "avoid it."

  • What's worth avoiding isn't determining right from wrong in an argument, but conflating that with a win or loss for the person
  • The barrier that most often drags a debate toward zero-sum is the defensive reflex of a threatened ego — recognizing that reflex is the first step to keeping a debate positive-sum
  • With a high-ego opponent, yielding is only meaningful as a deliberate choice; if it's just to avoid discomfort, it's a debt left for later
  • When the other side doesn't want a mutual win, Carnegie's original advice — avoid it — remains the wisest choice
