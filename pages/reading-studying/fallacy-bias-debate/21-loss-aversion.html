Loss Aversion

Losing something hurts more than gaining the same thing feels good

Updated: July 21, 2026

Definition

Loss aversion is the tendency to weigh losses more heavily than equivalent gains — losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good, even though the dollar amount is identical. It's not that people dislike risk in general; it's that the pain of a loss is measured from a reference point, usually whatever you already have, and drops below that point hurt more than equal-sized rises above it help.

Psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky formalized this in 1979 as part of Prospect Theory, their alternative to classical models of decision-making under risk. [1] Their original estimate put the effect at roughly two-to-one — a loss feels about twice as painful as an equivalent gain feels pleasant — though later research, described below, suggests that ratio moves around depending on the stakes and the situation.

Examples

Loss aversion shows up in a habit most investors recognize, a classic experiment involving a coffee mug, and a large-scale study of what real trading accounts actually do.

Holding On Until You "Break Even"

A stock drops 20% below what you paid for it, and selling suddenly feels much harder than it did when the stock was flat. Nothing about the investment's future prospects necessarily changed — what changed is that selling now would convert an abstract loss into a locked-in, official one, and that conversion is what loss aversion resists. The common workaround, "I'll sell once it gets back to what I paid," treats the original purchase price as the reference point to protect, rather than treating the money as fungible and asking a simpler question: would I buy this today, at today's price, if I didn't already own it?

The Mug Experiment

In a series of experiments published in 1990, Kahneman, Jack Knetsch, and Richard Thaler randomly handed half of a group of participants a coffee mug and then set up a market: mug owners named the lowest price they'd sell for, and everyone else named the highest price they'd pay to buy one. [2] Standard economic theory predicts owners and non-owners should value the mug about the same, so roughly half the mugs should change hands. Instead, owners demanded roughly twice what buyers were willing to offer, and far fewer mugs traded than predicted. Simply owning the mug, even for a few minutes, turned giving it up into a loss rather than a neutral trade — a related pattern now known as the endowment effect.

10,000 Brokerage Accounts

In 1998, finance professor Terrance Odean analyzed the trading records of 10,000 accounts at a large discount brokerage to test whether investors sell winning and losing stocks at different rates. [3] They did: investors were significantly more likely to sell a stock that had gone up than one that had gone down, holding onto losers noticeably longer than winners. This "disposition effect" wasn't explained by portfolio rebalancing, trading costs, or a reasonable belief that the losers would rebound — the sold winners went on to outperform the held losers over the following year, meaning the pattern cost these investors real, measurable money.

A Few Studies on Loss Aversion

Here's what happened when researchers tried to pin down exactly how large this effect is, and whether it holds up everywhere.

The study: In 2024, economists Alexander Brown, Taisuke Imai, Ferdinand Vieider, and Colin Camerer combined 607 separate estimates of the loss aversion coefficient from 150 published studies across economics, psychology, and neuroscience. [4]

The result: The pooled estimate landed close to Kahneman and Tversky's original figure — a mean coefficient of about 1.96, meaning losses are felt at roughly double the intensity of equivalent gains, with a 95% probability the true value sits between 1.82 and 2.10.

The complication: That average hides real disagreement underneath it. A separate line of research — including work by Eyal Ert, Ido Erev, and later Kate Mrkva — has found that for small stakes, loss aversion often shrinks close to zero or disappears entirely; people treat a small potential loss and an equal small potential gain as roughly neutral, and the effect becomes reliable mainly once the amounts involved start to matter. Mrkva's own 2020 response argued the effect is real and "alive and well" even at modest stakes, but conceded it depends heavily on the specific context and how the choice is framed.

Why this is useful: Loss aversion isn't a fixed constant you can multiply into every decision — it's strongest exactly where a loss feels concrete, personal, and final, which is precisely the moment (selling a losing stock, ending a bad project, walking away from a sunk cost) when it's most likely to steer you wrong.

Mitigation Strategies

Because loss aversion depends on which reference point you're measuring from, the fixes that work are the ones that change or widen that reference point before the decision, not after.

  • Ask the re-purchase question: For anything you're deciding whether to keep — a stock, a subscription, a project — ask "would I acquire this today, at today's terms, if I didn't already have it?" instead of "can I avoid locking in this loss?"
  • Set the rule before you're in the decision: Predetermined exit rules (a stop-loss price, a project review date) remove the in-the-moment choice, which is exactly when loss aversion is strongest and judgment is weakest
  • Zoom out to the whole portfolio, not one line item: Economists Shlomo Benartzi and Richard Thaler showed that evaluating investments less frequently — as a portfolio over years rather than a position checked daily — measurably reduces the loss aversion investors act on, because fewer individual losses ever come into view
  • Notice when "final" is doing the work, not "size": A loss that's still on paper and one that's been locked in by selling are financially identical but feel very different; naming that gap out loud is often enough to loosen its grip on the decision

Summary

Key Takeaway: Loss aversion means a loss is felt roughly twice as intensely as an equivalent gain, a pattern confirmed across hundreds of studies but strongest for large, concrete, personally-owned stakes and weaker or absent for small ones. It quietly shapes behavior anywhere a "loss" can be avoided by simply not acting — holding a falling stock, keeping a bad subscription, staying in a failing project — which is exactly why the fix is structural: pre-set rules and wider framing, not willpower applied in the moment.
