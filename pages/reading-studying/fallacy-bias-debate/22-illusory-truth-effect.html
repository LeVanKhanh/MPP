Illusory Truth Effect

Hear something enough times, and it starts to feel true regardless of whether it is

Updated: July 21, 2026

Definition

The illusory truth effect is the tendency to rate a repeated statement as more likely true than a statement you're hearing for the first time — even when the statement is false, and even when you actually know better. Repetition doesn't add evidence; what it adds is fluency, the feeling that information is easy to process. The brain uses that ease as a shortcut for truth, on the rough logic that things which are true tend to be familiar. Most of the time that shortcut works fine. It fails badly the moment something false gets repeated anyway.

The effect was first demonstrated in 1977 by psychologists Lynn Hasher, David Goldstein, and Thomas Toppino, who had participants rate the plausibility of 60 trivia-style statements across three separate sessions spaced two weeks apart. [1] A subset of statements reappeared across sessions while others didn't; the repeated ones, whether true or false, were consistently rated as more valid the second and third time around, purely from having been seen before.

Examples

The effect shows up in an advertising trick everyone half-recognizes, the original trivia-statement study, and a modern experiment that briefly seemed to disprove it before making it more troubling.

The Jingle You Can't Unhear

A slogan repeated across enough billboards, jingles, and commercials starts to feel less like a claim and more like a known fact — "melts in your mouth, not in your hands" or "the best a man can get" isn't accompanied by new evidence each time it airs, only more exposure. Advertisers don't need you to consciously agree with a slogan; they only need it to feel familiar enough that, at the moment of choosing between products, one option feels more like something you already trust.

Sixty Statements, Three Sessions, Two-Week Gaps

In Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino's original design, statements were deliberately chosen to be plausible but obscure enough that college students wouldn't already know whether they were true — things like historical trivia and factual claims about geography or science. [1] Participants never realized repetition was the manipulation being tested; they simply rated their confidence each session, unaware that their confidence in the repeated items was quietly climbing regardless of whether those statements were ever true.

Knowing Better Doesn't Help

In 2015, Lisa Fazio and colleagues tested whether the effect could be blocked by prior knowledge — using statements participants should already have known were false, like "a sari is the short skirt worn in Scotland" (participants had generally correctly identified this as a kilt, not a sari, beforehand). [2] Repeating the false statement still increased participants' belief in it, even among people who had the correct knowledge stored and available. The researchers called this "knowledge neglect": fluency from repetition can override what you actually know, rather than simply filling a gap in what you don't. A related 2018 study by Gordon Pennycook and colleagues found the same pattern with real fake news headlines shown on simulated social media feeds — a single prior exposure was enough to measurably raise a headline's perceived accuracy a week later, even for headlines flagged as disputed by fact-checkers. [3]

A Few Studies on the Illusory Truth Effect

Here's what a large synthesis of the research found once dozens of individual studies were combined.

The study: In 2010, Alice Dechêne and colleagues combined 51 separate studies of the truth effect published since the original 1977 paper, checking how consistent the effect was and what made it stronger or weaker. [4]

The result: The effect replicated reliably across the pooled studies — repetition reliably increased perceived truth — but its size depended heavily on how it was measured and under what conditions. Comparing a repeated statement to a brand-new one produced a bigger effect than comparing a statement's own rating the first time it appeared versus the second time; longer delays between repetitions, more repetitions, and vaguer or harder-to-verify statements all made the effect stronger.

The takeaway: This isn't a niche laboratory curiosity that only works on trivia nobody cares about — it's a general fluency effect that gets more dangerous, not less, in exactly the conditions that describe modern information feeds: short claims, frequent repetition, and topics too broad or novel for any one person to fact-check on the spot.

Why this is useful: The Fazio and Pennycook findings mean the usual defense — "I already know that's false, so repeating it can't fool me" — doesn't actually protect you. The moment to be skeptical isn't when you first hear a claim; it's every time you notice a claim feels obviously true and can't immediately say why.

Mitigation Strategies

Because the effect works through a feeling of fluency rather than through actual evidence, the fixes that work are the ones that force a check on the evidence itself, independent of how familiar a claim feels.

  • Separate the feeling from the fact: When something feels obviously true, pause and ask "did I verify this, or do I just remember hearing it somewhere" — familiarity and accuracy are different questions with different answers
  • Check the source every time, not just the first time: A claim doesn't earn more credibility by being repeated; if it wasn't well-sourced the first time you heard it, the tenth repetition hasn't fixed that
  • Be most cautious in high-repetition environments: Social media, advertising, and political messaging are built around repetition by design, which makes them the exact settings where this effect is doing the most quiet work
  • Expect one correction to not be enough: Because repeated falsehoods build up fluency over many exposures, a single correction rarely fully undoes the effect; counteracting a widely repeated claim usually takes its own repetition, not just one rebuttal

Summary

Key Takeaway: The illusory truth effect means repetition alone — with no new evidence attached — reliably makes a claim feel more true, and this holds even for people who already know the claim is false. It's strongest in exactly the environments built around repeating short claims, which makes "I'd know if that were false" an unreliable defense. The fix is to check sources and evidence independent of how familiar or obvious a claim feels, and to expect that undoing a widely repeated falsehood takes more than a single correction.
