In-Group Bias

We favor "our own" even when the group we've been sorted into means nothing at all

Updated: July 21, 2026

Definition

In-group bias is the tendency to favor members of your own group over outsiders — trusting them more, cooperating with them more readily, and judging their actions more generously — simply because they share your group membership. The unsettling part isn't that people favor their family, hometown, or company; it's how little a "group" has to mean before the favoritism kicks in.

Psychologist Henri Tajfel demonstrated this in 1971 with what became known as the minimal group paradigm: he sorted participants into groups using a coin flip or a stated preference between two abstract painters, groups that shared no history, no future, and no actual stake in each other's outcomes. [1] Even so, when given a chance to allocate real money between an anonymous member of their own group and an anonymous member of the other, participants consistently gave more to their own group — and often chose an option that maximized the gap between the groups over one that would have given their own group more money in absolute terms.

Examples

The bias shows up in a familiar hiring habit, the study that gave it its name, and a summer camp experiment that turned two groups of ordinary boys into rivals within days.

The Same School, the Same Old Team

A hiring manager reviewing two similar résumés often leans toward the candidate who went to the same university, worked at the same previous company, or was referred by a current employee — not because that overlap predicts better job performance, but because it quietly signals "one of us." Referral-based and alumni-network hiring runs on exactly this mechanism, and it's a large part of why professional networks tend to stay demographically similar to whoever already occupies them, generation after generation, without anyone making an explicit decision to exclude outsiders.

Klee or Kandinsky, and Then You Have a Tribe

In Tajfel's studies, participants were told they'd been grouped by their preference for one of two abstract painters — a categorization so trivial that participants had never met their supposed groupmates and would never interact with them again. [1] Yet in a follow-up allocation task, participants handed out points that determined real payouts, and the pattern was unmistakable: people gave their own group more, and were willing to accept a smaller total payout for their group if it meant a bigger gap over the other group. The label alone — Klee-lover or Kandinsky-lover — was enough to organize behavior into "us" and "them."

The Eagles and the Rattlers

In 1954, psychologist Muzafer Sherif took 22 boys, all strangers to each other, to a summer camp at Robbers Cave State Park and split them into two cabins. [3] Within days of bonding separately and naming themselves the Eagles and the Rattlers, the two groups — who'd had no prior reason to dislike one another — moved from teasing to raiding each other's cabins and burning each other's flags once organizers introduced competitive games between them. The hostility wasn't imported from outside; it was generated almost entirely by the act of dividing boys into two labeled groups and then giving them something to compete over. Sherif later found that only shared goals requiring both groups' cooperation — not contact or discussion alone — meaningfully reduced the conflict.

A Few Studies on In-Group Bias

Here's what a large-scale review found when it pooled decades of experiments on group favoritism.

The study: In 2014, Daniel Balliet, Junhui Wu, and Carsten De Dreu combined the results of studies testing whether people cooperate more with in-group members than with out-group members in economic games. [2]

The result: Across the pooled studies, people reliably cooperated more with in-group members than with out-group members, though the effect size was small to moderate (d = 0.32) rather than overwhelming. Crucially, the analysis found this was driven almost entirely by favoritism toward the in-group — actively helping "us" more — rather than by punishing or sabotaging the out-group; active hostility toward outsiders was a much weaker and less consistent finding than warmth toward insiders.

The takeaway: In-group bias is better described as a bias toward extra generosity for "us" than as active animosity toward "them." That distinction matters practically: most everyday in-group bias — favoring a former classmate's résumé, trusting a fellow alum's judgment more readily — looks like ordinary kindness and loyalty, which is exactly what makes it easy to miss as a bias at all.

Why this is useful: Because the effect is about who gets the benefit of the doubt rather than who gets actively harmed, the moments to watch for it are decisions about trust, credit, and opportunity — hiring, promotions, and informal favors — not just moments of open conflict between groups.

Mitigation Strategies

Because the bias runs on group labels rather than on anything about individual merit, the fixes that work are the ones that either hide the label or give people a bigger, shared group to belong to.

  • Remove the group cue from the decision: Structured, criteria-based evaluation and blind review — stripping names, schools, or affiliations before judging the substance of work — closes off the exact information the bias runs on
  • Create a shared goal that spans both groups: The Robbers Cave conflict only eased once the Eagles and Rattlers needed each other to solve a problem neither group could solve alone — a "superordinate goal" that made the old group line less relevant than the new shared one
  • Increase real contact across group lines: Structured collaboration between people from different groups, backgrounds, or departments has repeatedly been shown to reduce the favoritism gap over time, more reliably than one-off diversity training
  • Separate "extra warmth to us" from "the facts about them": Since the bias mainly shows up as generosity toward insiders rather than hostility toward outsiders, the practical check is asking whether you're giving someone the benefit of the doubt because of the evidence or because of the group they belong to

Summary

Key Takeaway: In-group bias needs almost nothing to activate — a coin flip or a painting preference is enough to trigger favoritism toward "our" group — and research shows it operates mainly as extra warmth toward insiders rather than active hostility toward outsiders, which is exactly why it so often passes for ordinary loyalty instead of being recognized as a bias. The fix is structural: remove group labels from evaluations where you can, and build shared goals and real contact across group lines where you can't.
