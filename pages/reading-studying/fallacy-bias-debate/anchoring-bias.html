Anchoring Bias

Over-reliance on first piece of information

Published: December 17, 2025

Definition

Anchoring bias is a cognitive bias where individuals rely too heavily on the first piece of information (the "anchor") they receive when making decisions, which can unduly influence subsequent judgments and estimates.

Examples

  • Price Negotiations: The initial price offered can set the tone for the entire negotiation, influencing subsequent offers and counteroffers
  • Salary Discussions: The first salary figure mentioned can anchor expectations for the entire negotiation
  • Real Estate: The listing price of a property can influence buyers' perceptions of its value
  • Legal Sentencing: Initial sentencing recommendations can anchor judges' final decisions

Causes

  • Cognitive Heuristics: The brain's tendency to use mental shortcuts for efficiency
  • Insufficient Adjustment: Failure to adequately adjust away from the anchor when making estimates

Consequences

  • Skewed Judgments: Decisions may be biased towards the anchor, leading to suboptimal outcomes
  • Reduced Objectivity: Overreliance on initial information can hinder critical evaluation of alternatives

Mitigation Strategies

  • Awareness and Education: Inform individuals about anchoring bias to promote self-awareness
  • Consider Multiple Anchors: Evaluate information from various sources to avoid overreliance on a single anchor
  • Deliberate Adjustment: Make conscious efforts to adjust estimates away from the anchor
  • Seek Objective Data: Base decisions on factual information rather than initial impressions

Key Takeaway

Anchoring bias shows how the first piece of information we receive can have a disproportionate influence on our decisions. By being aware of this effect and deliberately considering alternative reference points, we can make more balanced judgments.
