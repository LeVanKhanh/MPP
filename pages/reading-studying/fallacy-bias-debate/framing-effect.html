Framing Effect

The same facts, dressed in different words, lead to different decisions

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

The framing effect is the tendency to react differently to logically identical information depending on how it's worded — as a gain or a loss, a success rate or a failure rate. The facts underneath don't change; only the frame around them does. But that frame alone is often enough to flip which option people choose.

The key driver is loss aversion: losses generally feel more painful than equivalent gains feel good, so when a choice is framed in terms of what you stand to lose, people become more willing to gamble to avoid that loss; when the same choice is framed in terms of what you stand to gain, people become more cautious to protect it. Nothing about the actual odds changes — only which side of the ledger the words point you toward.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a corporate scandal it helps explain.

The Same Performance Review, Two Different Openings

Imagine two managers reviewing the same employee with the exact same data: the employee completed 90% of assigned work on time this quarter. The first manager opens the review with, "You hit 90% of your deadlines — that's strong work." The second opens with, "10% of your work missed deadline — we need to talk about that." Same number, but the employee walks out of those two meetings with completely different impressions of their own competence — not because their performance changed, but because the frame around it did.

The Disease Problem That Named the Effect

In 1981, Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman posed a now-famous hypothetical: the U.S. is preparing for an unusual disease expected to kill 600 people, and two prevention programs are on the table. One group of participants saw the programs described in terms of gains: Program A would save exactly 200 people; Program B had a one-third chance of saving all 600 and a two-thirds chance of saving no one. 72% chose Program A, the safe, certain option. A second group saw the mathematically identical programs described in terms of losses: Program C would result in exactly 400 people dying; Program D had a one-third chance no one would die and a two-thirds chance all 600 would die. This time, only 22% chose the certain option (Program C) — most switched to the risky one. [1] The two pairs of choices are mathematically identical — only the wording changed, and it flipped the majority's decision.

The Memo That Cost Ford $125 Million

In 1973, Ford's internal team drafted a cost-benefit analysis weighing whether to spend an extra $11 per car to fix a design flaw in the Pinto's fuel tank, which could rupture and catch fire in rear-end collisions. The memo calculated that not fixing the flaw would cost roughly $49.5 million — based on 180 projected burn deaths valued at $200,000 each, 180 serious burn injuries at $67,000 each, and 2,100 burned vehicles at $700 each — cheaper than the cost of fixing every car. Internally, this was framed as a routine engineering calculation, using the same formula federal regulators used to evaluate industry-wide safety rules. But when the memo surfaced during the 1981 lawsuit Grimshaw v. Ford Motor Co., the press and jury didn't read it as a "cost-benefit analysis" anymore — they read it as evidence that Ford had put a dollar value on human life and chosen to save money instead. [2] The exact same numbers, reframed from an internal engineering spreadsheet into "the price of a life," led the jury to award $125 million in punitive damages (later reduced to $3.5 million).

A Few Studies on the Framing Effect

Here's a study that tested whether the framing effect survives a more realistic test: actually tasting the product.

The study: In 1988, researchers Irwin Levin and Gary Gaeth wanted to test whether framing effects hold up once people get direct, physical evidence to check them against. They had two groups of participants evaluate the same ground beef — one group saw it labeled "75% lean," the other saw it labeled "25% fat." Then both groups actually tasted the cooked meat and rated it again. [3]

The result: Before tasting, the "75% lean" group rated the meat as noticeably tastier and higher quality than the "25% fat" group did — despite it being identical meat. After actually tasting it, the gap between the two groups narrowed substantially, but it didn't fully disappear.

The takeaway: Direct experience — tasting it yourself, checking it yourself — can weaken the framing effect, but isn't enough to erase it completely. Even when your own senses provide contradicting evidence, the initial impression created by how something was labeled leaves a residue.

Why it's useful: This tells you exactly when to be most on guard: before you have any direct experience to check a claim against — a flyer, a contract summary, a headline. That's the moment the frame has full control over your impression, because nothing else exists yet to test it against.

Mitigation Strategies

The framing effect works by exploiting which side of a gain/loss ledger the wording points to, so the fixes that work are the ones that strip the wording away and leave only the numbers.

  • Translate the number into its opposite frame: Before deciding, rewrite the information in the reverse direction — if you hear "90% survival," ask what "10% mortality" means — if both versions still lead you to the same choice, you're judging the facts, not the wording
  • Strip out the adjectives, keep only the raw number: "On time 90%" and "late 10%" — keep just the number (90 or 10) and discard the words chosen to trigger a feeling ("strong work" / "we need to talk")
  • Slow down extra hard when a loss frame appears: Because people take on far more risk to avoid a clearly stated loss — as in the disease problem above — pause longer than usual on any choice framed as "here's what you'll lose if you don't pick X"
  • Don't assume direct experience automatically fixes it: The ground beef study shows that even tasting the product yourself leaves a residue of the original frame — so re-read the original claim under a different frame instead of trusting that "trying it will make it obvious"

Summary

Key Takeaway: The framing effect shows that identical facts can produce opposite decisions depending on whether they're worded as a gain or a loss, because losses simply feel more painful than equivalent gains feel good. The real defense is translating a claim into its opposite frame before deciding, keeping only the raw numbers, and staying especially cautious around any pitch framed as what you stand to lose.
