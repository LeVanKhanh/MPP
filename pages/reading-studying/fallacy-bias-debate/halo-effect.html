Halo Effect

One good impression quietly paints every other trait in the same color

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

The halo effect is the tendency to let one positive (or negative) impression of a person, product, or company bleed into our judgment of their completely unrelated traits. Decide that someone is intelligent, and you'll likely also rate them as more honest, more competent, and even more attractive — not because you have any evidence for that, but because your brain doesn't naturally judge traits one at a time. It reaches for a single overall impression and paints everything else the same color.

Psychologist Edward Thorndike named the effect in 1920 after noticing something odd in how military officers rated their soldiers: traits that should have been completely independent — physique, intelligence, leadership, character — were rated as suspiciously correlated with each other. An officer seen as a strong leader was also rated as more intelligent and more reliable, often with no separate evidence for either. [1]

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a business reversal it helps explain.

Two Identical Essays, One Neater Handwriting

Imagine a teacher grading two essays with identical content, identical arguments, and the same number of spelling mistakes — the only difference is that one is written in neat handwriting and the other in a messy, hard-to-read scrawl. Even trying to grade only the content, the teacher tends to rate the neater essay's argument as stronger, "more coherent" — even though, word for word, the two essays don't differ at all. The impression from the handwriting quietly bled into the judgment of the content.

The Study That Caught the Bias Without Anyone Noticing

In 1977, psychologists Richard Nisbett and Timothy Wilson filmed two interviews with the exact same instructor, saying the same words, but in two different tones: warm and friendly in one, cold and distant in the other. They showed 118 students one of the two videos and asked them to rate traits that had nothing to do with personality — his physical appearance, his mannerisms, even his accent. Students who saw the "warm" version rated his appearance, mannerisms, and accent as appealing; students who saw the "cold" version rated those exact same physical traits as irritating — despite it being the same person, the same face, the same voice. [2] What stood out most: when asked whether their overall impression of his personality had influenced their rating of his appearance, most students insisted it hadn't — they genuinely had no idea they'd been influenced at all.

When Cisco Went From "Visionary" to "A Mess" Overnight

In the late 1990s, as Cisco Systems' stock soared during the dot-com boom, business journalists and researchers praised its strategy as "brilliant," its acquisition management as "masterful," and CEO John Chambers as "visionary." When the bubble burst and Cisco lost more than $400 billion in market value within 12 months, many of the same observers turned around and described those exact same practices — the same strategy, the same acquisition process, the same leadership style — as unfocused, chaotic, and poorly managed. [3] Researcher Phil Rosenzweig, who later dissected this whole episode, pointed out that most of what business journalism calls "strategy," "culture," and "leadership" isn't actually observed directly — it's inferred backward from the company's most recent financial results. When the stock is up, everything looks brilliant; when it's down, the exact same things suddenly look like mistakes.

A Few Studies on the Halo Effect

Here's a review that asked exactly which traits the halo effect actually reaches, and which ones it doesn't.

The study: In 1991, researchers Alice Eagly, Richard Ashmore, Mona Makhijani, and Laura Longo combined every published study on the "beautiful is good" stereotype — the common belief that physically attractive people also possess more positive qualities overall. They wanted to know: how strong is this stereotype, and is it equally strong for every kind of trait? [4]

The result: Attractive people were indeed rated more favorably across many traits — but the size of the effect varied enormously by trait type. It was strongest for judgments of "social competence" (being outgoing, socially skilled, confident in conversation); moderate for traits like assertiveness or intellectual competence; and close to zero for judgments of integrity or concern for others.

The takeaway: In other words, an attractive appearance makes people assume you're more socially skilled and confident — but it does not automatically make them assume you're more honest or kind. The halo effect doesn't spread evenly to every trait; it spreads strongest to traits closest to the original impression, and weakens the further a trait is from that impression.

Why it's useful: This tells you exactly where to be most on guard: when you're judging someone's social skill or confidence based on a first impression, you're likely under the halo effect's strongest influence. But it's also a reminder not to overuse this concept to dismiss every judgment — if you're assessing honesty or kindness specifically, the halo effect from appearance tends to matter far less than people usually assume.

Mitigation Strategies

The halo effect works by letting one overall impression stand in for a proper trait-by-trait evaluation, so the fixes that work are the ones that force separate, sequential judgments.

  • Rate one trait at a time, across everyone, before moving to the next: Score the same single criterion for every candidate or product before moving to the next criterion, instead of forming one "overall" judgment of each person at a time
  • Hide the information that triggers the halo: Where possible, remove names, photos, or the company's most recent financial results before evaluating skill or strategy — this is exactly what caused business journalism to completely reverse its read on Cisco based purely on the stock price
  • Don't trust the feeling of being objective: The Nisbett-Wilson study shows people under the halo effect's influence usually have no idea it's happening — so don't rely on "I feel like I'm being fair" as evidence; use a written, specific checklist to check yourself instead
  • Ask how close the trait you're judging is to your original impression: Per Eagly and colleagues' research, the halo effect reaches hardest for traits closest to the initial impression — if you're inferring something further away (like jumping from "charismatic speaker" to "honest"), remind yourself that the longer the inferential leap, the less grounded it is

Summary

Key Takeaway: The halo effect means one overall impression — of a person, a product, or a company's stock price — quietly colors judgments about completely unrelated traits, and research shows this happens without people noticing it in themselves. The real defense is rating one trait at a time across every option before moving to the next, hiding the information that triggers the halo, and staying most skeptical exactly when a trait you're judging is far removed from the original impression that formed it.
