Self-Serving Bias

Success is "my talent," failure is "bad luck" — and the pattern shows up on paper too

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Self-serving bias is the tendency to credit our successes to our own ability and effort while blaming our failures on circumstances outside our control — bad luck, unfair conditions, other people. The exact same person, judging the exact same kind of outcome, applies two different standards of explanation depending only on whether the result was good or bad.

Part of this comes from self-protection: crediting yourself for wins and blaming the world for losses keeps self-esteem intact. But part of it is more mundane: when you work hard and expect to succeed, success just confirms what you predicted, so it needs no further explanation — while failure is surprising and violates the story you expected, so the mind goes looking for something else to explain it away. Either way, the asymmetry is consistent and well documented, well beyond individual psychology.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and a financial collapse it helps explain.

The Sales Team's Two Different Stories About the Same Quarter

Imagine a sales team just finished a quarter that beat its target. In the wrap-up meeting, the team lead says, "we worked hard, and our outreach strategy really paid off." Next quarter, sales drop. The same team lead says, "the market's rough right now, competitors slashed prices, nobody could've predicted it." Same team, same lead — the only difference is the good result gets credited to internal ability, and the bad result gets blamed on external circumstances. No one is lying on purpose; it's simply the natural way people narrate their own story.

The Study That Read the Sports Pages

In 1980, researchers Richard Lau and Dan Russell analyzed 107 newspaper articles across 8 major papers, recording 594 total explanations offered by players, coaches, and sportswriters for why a team won or lost baseball and football games. The result: most explanations for winning cited internal factors — talent, effort, preparation — while explanations for losing leaned heavily on external factors like injuries, weather, or bad luck. [1] Notably, the players and coaches themselves showed this pattern far more strongly than the sportswriters covering them: 80.3% of players' and coaches' explanations for a win cited internal factors, compared to only 42.9% among sportswriters — meaning the people with the most personal stake in looking good showed the clearest bias.

The CEO Who Blamed Everyone but the Firm's Own Leverage

In October 2008, just weeks after Lehman Brothers — one of Wall Street's largest investment banks — collapsed in the largest bankruptcy filing in U.S. history, CEO Richard Fuld testified before Congress. He attributed the collapse to a long list of external forces: "naked" short sellers, credit rating agencies, credit default swaps spiraling out of control, and a "rumor mill" that eroded confidence in the firm. As late as his 2010 testimony before the Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission, Fuld still insisted Lehman had been adequately capitalized and would have survived if the government had intervened — even though internal records showed that just days before the bankruptcy, he genuinely believed the firm was "fully capitalized." [2] None of these explanations mentioned the extreme leverage and risky subprime asset holdings that Lehman's own leadership had chosen to pursue for years beforehand.

A Few Studies on Self-Serving Bias

Here's a study that checked whether this bias survives in a company's most carefully edited official documents, not just spontaneous speech.

The study: In 1983, researchers Barry Staw, Pamela McKechnie, and Sheila Puffer analyzed shareholder letters in corporate annual reports, to see whether self-serving bias shows up even in an official, carefully edited business document — not just an individual's off-the-cuff remarks. [3]

The result: They found clear evidence: when a company performed well, its shareholder letter credited strategy and leadership; when it performed poorly, the letter pointed to macroeconomic conditions or industry-wide headwinds. More surprisingly, this self-serving framing actually worked on investors — the company's stock price tended to rise afterward. But at the same time, the periods when leadership praised itself most heavily in shareholder letters also tended to coincide with those same executives quietly selling their own personal shares soon after.

The takeaway: That last detail matters: it suggests this wasn't simply leadership's honest optimism about their own company — it looked more like a deliberate impression-management tactic, saying one thing to investors while acting differently with their own money.

Why it's useful: This is useful because it gives you a concrete way to check any self-assessment — from a shareholder letter to a personal performance review: don't just look at the explanation, look at the accompanying behavior. If someone confidently praises their own results in words while quietly acting as if they don't believe it, that gap is worth far more attention than the explanation itself.

Mitigation Strategies

Self-serving bias works by applying a different explanatory standard to success than to failure, so the fixes that work are the ones that force the same standard both ways.

  • Apply the exact same question to both success and failure: After any outcome, good or bad, ask the identical question — "what percentage came from internal factors (my ability, my decisions) versus external factors (luck, circumstance)?" — don't change the question depending on whether the result was good or bad
  • Look at behavior, not just the explanation: The shareholder-letter study shows self-praise can contradict actual behavior (like leadership quietly selling shares) — when judging anyone's explanation of a success, including your own, check whether the accompanying actions actually match the words
  • Be extra skeptical of whoever has the most to protect: The sports-pages study shows the people with the most personal stake (players, coaches) were far more biased than outside observers (sportswriters) — when you need an objective read on why something succeeded or failed, prioritize asking someone with nothing to gain or lose from the answer
  • Write the post-mortem before knowing who will read it: To reduce the motive for self-protection, write an assessment of a project's success or failure as if no one else will ever see it — purely for your own learning — which takes the pressure off telling a flattering story

Summary

Key Takeaway: Self-serving bias applies one explanatory standard to success and a different one to failure, and research shows this happens even in official corporate documents, not just casual speech — sometimes contradicted by the very actions of the people making the claim. The real defense is asking the identical question regardless of outcome, checking behavior against explanation, and trusting outside observers with nothing at stake over the people who have the most reason to protect their own story.
