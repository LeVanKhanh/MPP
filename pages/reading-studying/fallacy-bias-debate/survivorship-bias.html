Survivorship Bias

We study the winners and never see the far larger pile of losers who did the exact same thing

Updated: July 20, 2026

Definition

Survivorship bias is the tendency to draw conclusions from a group of successes — the survivors — while ignoring the far larger group that attempted the same thing and failed, and therefore left no visible trace to study. Because failures tend to quietly disappear while successes stay visible and get studied, celebrated, and copied, we end up mistaking traits the survivors happen to share for the actual cause of their success — without ever checking whether the failures shared those exact same traits too.

Examples

The bias shows up at three different levels of reality: a scenario you can picture, a study that measured it, and an entire industry it helps explain.

The Champion's Morning Routine

Imagine an article interviews five Olympic gold medalists and discovers all five share one habit: waking up at 4 a.m. to train. The article concludes: waking up early is the secret behind winning gold. But no one went and asked the tens of thousands of other athletes — the ones who also woke up at 4 a.m. for years, trained just as hard, and never came close to a medal. If most serious athletes wake up early, the habit may have nothing to do with who wins — it might just be a baseline requirement for being taken seriously at all, not the cause of success.

The Study That Named the Bias

During World War II, the U.S. military studied bombers that returned from missions, charting where bullet holes clustered on the fuselage, intending to add armor to the areas hit most often. Mathematician Abraham Wald, working with the Statistical Research Group at Columbia University, pointed out a fundamental flaw: all of that data came only from planes that survived to return — the ones hit hardest had been shot down before they could make it back, and were completely absent from the data. [1] Wald argued the opposite: reinforce exactly the areas where returning planes showed no damage, because a hit there was the kind that kept a plane from ever making it home to be studied at all. The military adopted his recommendation, and the approach later became the foundation of an entire field of research into this exact bias.

The Mutual Fund Industry's Disappearing Losers

In 1995, economist Burton Malkiel analyzed the performance of every U.S. equity mutual fund from 1971 to 1991 — including funds that had already shut down, which most financial industry databases at the time typically left out. He found that roughly 3% of funds disappeared from the market every year, almost always the worst performers, usually merged quietly into another fund or simply closed. [2] Because industry performance rankings and average returns are usually calculated only from funds still around at the time of the count, the "mutual fund industry" looked considerably better on paper than what an actual investor picking a random fund had really experienced — simply because the worst funds had already quietly vanished from the data before anyone got around to averaging it.

A Few Studies on Survivorship Bias

Here's what happened when a hugely influential business bestseller's own "formula for success" was checked against the companies it never looked at.

The study: In 1982, authors Tom Peters and Robert Waterman published the bestselling business book In Search of Excellence, studying 43 American companies performing exceptionally well at the time to distill 8 management principles supposedly behind their success — the book sold millions of copies and shaped corporate management thinking for decades afterward.

The result: Within just a few years of publication, most of those "excellent" companies had declined significantly or run into serious trouble — many of the publicly traded ones in the group underperformed the broader market in the years that followed. [3] The fundamental problem: the book selected only companies that were currently succeeding at one moment in time, then looked for traits they had in common — without ever checking whether failing or mediocre companies shared those exact same "successful" traits.

The takeaway: If a trait (like "a bias for action" or "staying close to the customer") shows up in failing companies at roughly the same rate as in successful ones, that trait doesn't predict success at all — it's just common, not causal. Because the book never looked at the failure group to compare, no one could know for sure whether those 8 principles actually caused excellence, or were simply what any large company at the time happened to be doing.

Why it's useful: This is useful because it tells you exactly what question to ask before trusting any "formula for success" drawn from studying winners: does the supposed secret ingredient show up at a similar rate among the people or companies that failed? If no one checked that, the formula isn't proven — it's only describing the winners, not explaining why they won.

Mitigation Strategies

Survivorship bias works by hiding the failure group from view entirely, so the fixes that work are the ones aimed at actively finding it.

  • Always ask "who else did the exact same thing and didn't make it?": Before trusting a success formula drawn from studying winners, actively go looking for the group that used the same approach and failed — if you can't find that group, treat the formula as unproven
  • Be wary of any data that only counts what's still around: As with the mutual fund case, ask whether a dataset or ranking includes the cases that disappeared, closed, or got dropped — if not, the average is almost certainly inflated
  • Look for exactly where the failures went missing: Wald's insight was to find the specific place where the data was silently absent — when analyzing any system, ask what part of reality quietly left your field of view before you had a chance to measure it
  • Don't learn only from the winners' story: When studying a field — business, investing, a career path — actively seek out the stories of people who tried the same path and failed; the difference between the two groups, not the traits unique to the winners alone, is the lesson actually worth trusting

Summary

Key Takeaway: Survivorship bias means the data we can see — winning athletes, surviving planes, still-open funds, thriving companies — is never a random sample of reality, because failures tend to disappear quietly and stop being counted. The real defense is actively hunting for the invisible failure group before trusting any lesson drawn from studying winners, and checking whether a dataset counts what vanished, not just what remains.
