Delegation

Effective strategies for delegating tasks and empowering your team

Three Main Reasons for Delegation

  • Your team will reach better results.
  • It lessens your burden and frees time for strategic work.
  • It helps other people grow.

Which Tasks to Delegate

  • Low effort - High skill: Often do these yourself; low effort, high expertise—can be a good use of your time.
  • High effort - High skill: Delegate as longer projects to skilled people (or those who can be trained). Expect more involvement early, less later.
  • Low effort - Low skill: Delegate by default; many can do these and keeping them yourself wastes time.
  • High effort - Low skill: Delegate when possible to lower-competence team members as stepping stones for them and for your delegation practice.

Four Questions Before Delegating

  • Is there someone who has (or can be given) the required knowledge or experience?
  • Does the task offer growth or skill development for another person?
  • Will this task recur in this or a similar form?
  • Can you make time to delegate effectively (train, support, check progress, give feedback)?

Who to Delegate To

  • Competence level.
  • Current workload.
  • Level of independence and ambition.

Expect initial tasks to take longer for someone new than if you did them yourself.

The Five Steps of Effective Delegation

  1. Specify outcome.
  2. Set guidelines (framework).
  3. Provide resources.
  4. Agree on evaluation/monitoring.
  5. Offer feedback and recognition.

The Four Delegation Styles

  • Instruct: For low-skill tasks; will may be high, skill is low.
  • Train: For some skill present; will may be lower, knowledge stronger.
  • Coach: For higher competence; guide rather than direct.
  • Monitor: For highest competence; delegate results, not methods—let them choose how.
