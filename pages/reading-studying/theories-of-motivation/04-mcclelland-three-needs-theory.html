McClelland's Three Needs Theory

Power, Achievement, and Affiliation as drivers of motivation

Published: December 17, 2025

Overview

McClelland's theory identifies three primary motivational drivers that vary in individuals: the need for Achievement, the need for Power, and the need for Affiliation. Understanding these needs helps explain workplace behavior and performance.

Need for Achievement (nACH)

Characterized by personal responsibility, feedback, and moderate risk-taking.

High nACH Behaviors

  • Must win at any cost
  • Must be on top and receive credit
  • Seeks challenging goals and measurable success

Low nACH Behaviors

  • Fears failure
  • Avoids responsibility

Need for Power (nPOW)

Characterized by the desire to influence others and engage in competitive situations.

High nPOW Behaviors

  • Demands blind loyalty and harmony
  • Does not tolerate disagreement
  • Desires control of everyone and everything
  • Exaggerates own position and resources

Low nPOW Behaviors

  • Remains aloof
  • Maintains social distance

Need for Affiliation (nAFF)

Characterized by the desire for acceptance, friendship, and cooperative relationships.

High nAFF Behaviors

  • Seeks harmonious relationships
  • Values cooperation and teamwork
  • Desires social acceptance and belonging

Low nAFF Behaviors

  • Dependent or subordinate
  • Minimizes own position and resources
