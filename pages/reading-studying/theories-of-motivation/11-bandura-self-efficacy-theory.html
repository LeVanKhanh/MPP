Bandura's Self-Efficacy Theory

Belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations

Published: December 17, 2025

Overview

Psychologist Albert Bandura has defined self-efficacy as people's belief in their ability to control their functioning and events that affect their lives. This belief system plays a crucial role in how people think, behave, and feel in various situations.

One's sense of self-efficacy can provide the foundation for motivation, well-being, and personal accomplishment. When individuals believe they can succeed, they are more likely to take on challenging tasks and persist in the face of obstacles.

Four Sources of Self-Efficacy

People's beliefs in their efficacy are developed by four primary sources of influence:

  1. Mastery Experiences: The most effective source of self-efficacy. Successfully completing tasks builds a strong belief in one's capabilities, while failures can undermine it, especially if they occur before confidence is established.
  2. Vicarious Experiences: Observing others successfully complete a task can strengthen beliefs in one's own abilities to accomplish similar activities. This is especially powerful when the observer identifies with the model.
  3. Social Persuasion: When others express faith in one's abilities, it can strengthen self-efficacy beliefs. However, it's easier to weaken self-efficacy through negative feedback than to strengthen it through encouragement alone.
  4. Emotional and Physiological States: Stress reactions and tension can be interpreted as signs of vulnerability to poor performance. Positive mood enhances self-efficacy, while anxiety and stress can diminish it.

Benefits of High Self-Efficacy

High self-efficacy has numerous benefits to daily life, including:

  • Resilience to adversity and stress: People with high self-efficacy are better able to bounce back from setbacks and view difficulties as challenges to overcome rather than threats to avoid.
  • Healthy lifestyle habits: Strong self-efficacy beliefs support the adoption and maintenance of health-promoting behaviors.
  • Improved employee performance: Workers with high self-efficacy set higher goals, are more committed to them, and respond better to negative feedback.
  • Educational achievement: Students with high self-efficacy are more likely to engage in challenging academic tasks and persist through difficulties.

Key Insight

Self-efficacy is not a fixed trait but a dynamic belief system that can be developed and strengthened over time. By understanding the four sources of self-efficacy, individuals and organizations can intentionally build confidence and capability through targeted experiences, role models, encouragement, and stress management techniques.
