5. Health and Clarity of Mind

Physical and mental foundations for sustainable value creation

Published: December 30, 2025

Endurance comes from balancing pressure and recovery

A steel bar, if used continuously without proper tempering or rest, will quickly lose its durability and deform. At first, it can still bear weight, even loads beyond what was planned. But the more it is pushed past its limits, the clearer the risk of breaking becomes. Not because the steel is low quality, but because it is used beyond its own load-bearing capacity.

The blacksmith understands this well. To forge a durable bar, he cares not only about how hard to strike, but also when to heat, when to cool, and when to stop. Endurance does not come from unceasing pressure, but from a balance between stress and recovery. A properly tempered bar may not impress at first glance, but it can bear steady loads for a long time.

Health and clarity are the conditions that allow intention to be sustained

People are no different. Health and clarity of mind do not directly create value, but they determine how much strain a person can endure under prolonged pace and pressure. When that foundation weakens, every effort—whether born of goodwill, discipline, or aspiration—becomes fragile. Value, in that case, does not collapse for lack of good intent, but for lack of the conditions that allow that intent to be sustained.

This chapter addresses those foundational conditions: health as the body’s load-bearing capacity, and clarity of mind as the ability to keep action from veering off course. Before talking about doing better, faster, or more, we need to know whether we have enough strength and alertness to keep acting rightly over a long enough stretch of time.

Continuity of action requires a minimal physical base

We can also look back at Creating Value Through Actions. When I used the image of having to “run” every morning—whether lion or gazelle—the focus was not on speed or competition, but on continuity of action. But to “run” day after day, the prerequisite is a body strong enough to bear that rhythm. Without a minimal physical foundation, any call for discipline or persistence becomes fragile.

Health as the quiet foundation of clarity

Even on a spiritual path, health remains indispensable. In Buddhism, the story of Prince Siddhartha Gautama’s six years of ascetic practice is a clear illustration. After years of self-mortification in pursuit of enlightenment, his body became weakened, nearly depleted of life force. In that state, not only was the body damaged, but the mind’s clarity also diminished.

The moment Sujata offered a bowl of milk porridge—a nourishing meal sufficient to restore some strength—marked an important turning point. As the body was nourished again, the mind gradually became clearer. In that restored clarity, he realized that extreme asceticism did not lead to liberation, but was another form of deviation. From that regained lucidity, the idea of the Middle Way—avoiding both indulgence and self-mortification—truly took shape.

This story suggests something worth considering: even the pursuit of ultimate truth cannot be separated from basic bodily conditions. When the body is pushed to its limits, the mind struggles to maintain the clarity needed to recognize the right path. In this sense, health does not oppose spiritual life; it is the quiet foundation that allows awakening to occur.

Placed in the context of creating value, the lesson becomes clearer. A person cannot sustain right action over the long term—whether in labor, creativity, or practice—if their body lacks the strength to support those efforts. Health, therefore, is not indulgence or self-pampering, but a necessary condition to avoid extremes that exhaust both body and mind.

Intellect keeps action from veering off course

If health is the physical base that allows people to keep acting, then intellect—the clarity of mind—is the cognitive base that keeps those actions on track. A sound body lets us stand up to the rhythm of life, but only a clear mind helps us understand where we are going and why we chose that path.

In distinguishing right from wrong, ethical from unethical, or value from non-value, intellect plays a pivotal role. When perception is clouded, people easily drift—not necessarily from ill will, but because they can no longer see the consequences of their actions. In such a state, what we create can become meaningless or even harmful to ourselves and those around us.

Conversely, when the mind is clear, a person can make decisions for themselves without being swept away by pressure, emotion, or external expectations. That clarity allows action with fuller awareness of consequences, and therefore a readiness to accept the results—or the responsibility—for what one creates. In this sense, intellect is not merely the ability to think clearly, but the condition that enables a person to truly own their life and the value they create.

Intellect also means not acting when no value is created

In Dale Carnegie’s How to Win Friends and Influence People, the opening chapter recounts a story about Abraham Lincoln during the American Civil War. When General Meade missed the chance to destroy the opposing army and allowed them to withdraw safely, Lincoln was deeply disappointed and intended to write a harsh letter of criticism. In the end, he did not send it. Carnegie uses this story to illustrate a familiar principle: “If you want to gather honey, don’t kick over the beehive.”

Seen through the lens of intellect and value creation, Lincoln’s decision shows sober consideration. Criticism might have helped him release immediate emotion, but it would not create any lasting value. It risked damaging the relationship, undermining cooperation, and reducing a key partner’s motivation. By restraining his emotional reaction and choosing not to act in anger, Lincoln preserved an important resource—a general who continued to serve the country in a critical period.

This story shows that intellect is not only the ability to act correctly, but also the ability not to act when that action creates no value. In many cases, pausing, restraining oneself, and choosing not to do something—though fully possible—can be the wisest decision, allowing value to be preserved and continue to grow over time.

From intellect to recognizing cognitive traps

I will explore the topic of intellect more deeply in the next chapter, “Common Traps in Value Creation.” There, I will focus on frequent distortions in perception and reasoning—blind spots that make people think they are creating value while actually veering off course. By recognizing these cognitive traps, the goal is not absolute correctness, but learning to think more clearly and cautiously before acting.

I do not emphasize intelligence as an inborn ability, because that is not something one can choose or decide. What I care more about is how people can gradually improve their own thinking—a capacity that can be trained, accumulated, and carried throughout life. In the journey of creating value, the ability to think clearly and correctly is the most durable companion, lasting longer than any other skill or tool.

When foundational conditions are insufficient, value is easily distorted

In the end, the problem is not that people lack value or goodwill, but that they often overestimate their intent and underestimate the conditions needed to live fully with that intent. When the body is not strong enough and the mind not clear enough, value—even when rightly chosen—can be distorted in the very process of being created.
