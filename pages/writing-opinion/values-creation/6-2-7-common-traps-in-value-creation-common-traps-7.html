6. Common Traps in Value Creation - Thinking Traps

Published: January 29, 2026

Anchoring Bias

Anchoring bias occurs when we rely too heavily on the first piece of information we receive, and unconsciously use it as a reference point for later judgments. Even when the initial information is no longer relevant, it continues to influence how we think and decide.

Framing Effect

The framing effect appears when the way a problem is presented changes our perception of that very problem. The same information, framed positively or negatively, can lead us to completely different decisions.

Bandwagon Effect

The bandwagon effect is the tendency to believe a choice is right simply because many others also believe it or follow it. In this context, the sense of safety in “going with the crowd” can override independent and rational evaluation.

Hindsight Bias

Hindsight bias makes us believe that outcomes that already happened were “something we should have foreseen.” After knowing the result, we often overestimate our past ability to predict, and draw unfair conclusions from it.

Optimism Bias

Optimism bias is the tendency to underestimate risks and overestimate the likelihood of positive outcomes for ourselves. This makes us more likely to ignore unfavorable scenarios and prepare inadequately for bad situations.

Status Quo Bias

Status quo bias makes us tend to keep things as they are, even when other options might be better. Familiarity often creates a sense of safety, causing us to delay or avoid change.

Overconfidence Bias

Overconfidence bias occurs when we overestimate our knowledge, skills, or ability to control situations. This can lead to hasty decisions and underestimating uncertainty.

Halo Effect

The halo effect makes us generalize from one prominent trait to judge an entire person, idea, or product. A positive first impression can obscure other aspects that have not been verified.

Selective Perception

Selective perception is the tendency to focus only on information that fits existing beliefs, while ignoring or downplaying conflicting data. This gradually narrows our perspective without us noticing.

Outline summary notes

  • Anchoring bias: over-reliance on first information.
  • Framing effect: presentation changes decisions.
  • Bandwagon effect: believing because many do.
  • Hindsight bias: “should have known” after the fact.
  • Optimism bias: underestimating risks for oneself.
  • Status quo bias: sticking to the familiar.
  • Overconfidence bias: overestimating control or skill.
  • Halo effect: generalizing from a first impression.
  • Selective perception: seeing only belief-consistent data.
